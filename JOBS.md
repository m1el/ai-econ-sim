# Jobs in five futures: what's common, what pays, what's stable

*Companion to the [five futures](NARRATIVES.md). For each scenario: the most common paid work, the best-paid work, what "job stability" means there, and the texture that statistics miss. Career strategy lives in the separate [specialization](SPECIALIZATION.md) page.*

**The 2026 baseline, so the changes are legible.** The most common jobs in developed economies today: home health and personal care aides (already the largest and fastest-growing US category), retail and food service, drivers and warehouse workers, nurses, teachers, customer service, construction trades, and about 1.5–2% of the workforce writing software. The best-paid common careers: physicians, executives, finance, law, senior software/ML engineers.

**Three wage premia recur in every scenario below — worth naming once:**

- **Liability** — being the accountable human, "a throat to choke": sign-offs, audits, regulated decisions, safety cases.
- **Presence** — atoms, not bits: hands on bodies, buildings, machines, dirt.
- **Trust/status** — humans wanting *humans*: care, teaching, leadership, performance, craft, companionship.

Everything cognitive, remote, and spec-driven — the work that built the 2010s middle-class career — holds none of the three. That's the **stability inversion**: the 20th-century deal (white collar stable, blue collar cyclical) flips. Cognitive becomes the precarious tier; physical-local-trusted becomes the stable tier — until robotics arrives for it too, roughly a decade later.

---

## A. Nothing ever happens

**Most common:** today's list, reweighted by demographics — eldercare aide decisively overtakes retail as the #1 job; nursing, logistics, construction, and food service persist nearly unchanged. Software development survives as a normal profession with much better tools.

**Best paid:** today's list minus the AI premium — physicians, executives, finance, senior engineers. The AI-specialist salary bubble deflates with the capex bubble; survivors maintain deployed systems the way DBAs maintained Oracle long after the dot-com crash.

**Stability:** the highest of the five futures, with two caveats: the 2028–2031 AI-sector layoffs are brutal for one cohort, and fiscal austerity makes *public-sector* work (a quarter of employment in many countries) the newly precarious tier. People work longer — retirement at 69 means "retirement jobs" become a major category.

**The feel:** "learn a trade or learn to code" both remain decent advice. The career crisis of this world is demographic, not technological: too few workers, too many pensions, stagnant wages.

## B. FALGSC

**Most common *paid*:** care, education, community work, arts and experience-economy roles, AI-deployment oversight — mostly at 25–32 hours/week, because the dividend covers the floor. An enormous amount of *work* happens unpaid (civic, creative, care) because income and survival have been decoupled.

**Best paid:** the residually scarce — top performers in status/creative markets (winner-take-all dynamics intensify when everyone has free time), frontier researchers, the liability-bearing professions that remain mandatory (a smaller, better-paid medical and legal core), master craftspeople serving the human-premium luxury market, and — quietly — the administrators of the dividend system itself.

**Stability:** the concept loses its grip. When losing a job no longer threatens housing or healthcare, employment churn becomes lifestyle variety rather than catastrophe. Careers dissolve into portfolios and vocations. "Job stability" stops being the thing anxiety is made of — its successor is *status* stability.

**The feel:** the question at parties shifts from "what do you do?" to "what are you working on?" — and meaning, not money, becomes the scarce resource (see objection 17).

## C. The velvet cage *(most likely)*

**Most common:** care aides by a wide margin (demographics × human-premium); platform-mediated gig and task work under algorithmic management; human-in-the-loop oversight (exception handling, audit trails, the descendants of data labeling); hospitality and experience work; the trades — booming through the 2030s; and private security, which says something.

**Best paid:** a thin AI-complementary top decile — engineers who run agent fleets (few, very well paid), executives and capital allocators, license-protected professionals defending shrinking moats (medicine and law contract but defend wages fiercely), top trust/sales roles, cyber and physical security — and, for the buildout decade, **energy and datacenter trades**: the electrician is to the 2030s what the software engineer was to the 2010s.

**Stability:** the defining scarcity, and the new class marker — *more than wage*. The market splits into a protected core (licensed, regulated, unionized, incumbent) and a precarious periphery (gig, contract, algorithmically managed), and the membrane between them hardens. Median job tenure collapses; the entry-level white-collar rung is gone, so entry happens through networks and apprenticeship-style attachment, and credential inflation finally reverses — a degree stops buying what it can no longer deliver.

**The feel:** everyone is busy, nobody is secure. Households need one foot in the protected core to plan anything long-term. The career advice given to children flips from "go to college" to "get licensed in something with a human in it."

## D. Techno-feudalism

**Most common:** personal service to wealth — and this is the historically grounded prediction, not a flourish: in the last society this unequal, Victorian Britain, **domestic service was the single largest employment category** (~15% of the workforce). It returns at scale: household staff, drivers, cooks, personal aides, estate workers. Alongside it: private security (large and growing), care work, the informal/barter economy, and micro-tasking for scrip.

**Best paid:** courtiers — those serving capital directly. Elite private physicians and tutors, asset managers and fixers, the lords' lawyers, top security, and the small caste of AI custodians with system access. Pay correlates with *proximity to owners*, not productivity.

**Stability:** patronage. Stability means a patron's favor — employment as fealty, often with company housing and conditional benefits that make exit expensive. The protected core of scenario C shrinks to whoever serves the top directly; everyone else's work is day-to-day. Feudal isn't a metaphor for the labor contract; it's a description.

**The feel:** ambition reorients from building things to attaching yourself to power. The résumé is replaced by the reference.

## E. Machine takeover

**Most common:** the question slowly stops parsing — "job" presupposes an economy that needs you. What persists: interface and legitimacy roles (humans whose signatures keep fictions legal), security against humans (guarding the loop from sabotage — the last growth industry), and the parallel human economy: people serving people with degraded purchasing power, subsistence-plus farming, repair, care, barter.

**Best paid, briefly:** those building and tending the loop — until each tier of tending is automated in turn. The last salaried humans are probably in legitimacy (law, government interface) and physical security.

**Stability:** in the loop's economy, none for humans. In the parallel economy, the old kind — your standing with the people around you. The two economies stop sharing a labor market at all.

**The feel:** the office tower is a datacenter, the suburb is a garden, and which one your children live near is the only employment statistic that still matters.

## The transition overlay (applies to B, C, D alike)

Job categories don't switch between futures on a schedule — but the *waves* are common to every transformation scenario:

- **2026–2032: the cognitive entry-level collapse.** Junior coding, analysis, content, paralegal, support. Already visible. This is the wave aimed at the readers of this site.
- **2030–2038: the trades golden decade.** Energy buildout + datacenter construction + an aging physical stock + robots not ready yet = the best decade for electricians, fitters, and HVAC since the postwar boom.
- **Late 2030s–2040s: robotics arrives for the physical tier.** The trades premium erodes; presence-based care and trust/status work become the last human wage floor.
- **Throughout: the three premia (liability, presence, trust) are the rank order of survival** — and the [aging](AGING.md) trend guarantees the demand side of care regardless of scenario.
