# Objections: the holes people poke in every comfortable story

*Companion to the [five futures](NARRATIVES.md). Every scenario there leans on some reassuring mechanism — "capital needs customers," "voters will demand a share," "physics is slow." This page collects the standard objections to those mechanisms: the two we find most load-bearing in full, then the wider catalog. For each: what comfortable claim it attacks, why it bites, and what's left of the comfort after it does.*

*Two forces that cut across nearly every entry have their own pages: [the demographic undertow](AGING.md) (how aging interacts with all five futures) and [the threat point](VIOLENCE.md) (how violence from the populace is priced in).*

## 1. The breakaway economy

**The comfortable claim it attacks:** *"Capital ultimately needs customers. A fortune in robots is worthless without people who can afford the output, so the rich must keep the rest of us solvent — consumer demand is your structural protection."*

**The objection.** That claim quietly assumes the economy's purpose is final consumption by humans. But an economy can run on *investment demand*: the AI sector buying compute, energy, land, and robots from itself in order to build more capacity. Once the loop is self-financing (its revenue funds its expansion), self-building (machines make machines), and self-directing (allocation run by AI with ceremonial sign-off), the customer the system needs is *itself*. There are working precedents at small scale: wartime economies and gold rushes both ran for years on investment demand with consumer welfare as an afterthought — and a meaningful slice of GDP growth in 2025–26 is already AI firms buying datacenters from AI-adjacent firms, justified by future AI revenue. In the limit, GDP grows spectacularly while the share of output that touches a human falls toward zero. Nobody is attacked; everybody is outbid for energy, land, and matter by a counterparty with a better balance sheet.

**Why it's strong.** It requires no villain and no coordination — competition *selects* for it. The firm that lets its AI reinvest and self-direct compounds faster than the firm that pauses for human review. Ownership drifts toward entities that never consume, only compound: index funds, treasuries, AI-managed portfolios. Each step is locally rational and individually defensible.

**What's left of the comfort.** Physical closure is the long pole — robots building fabs, mines, and power plants lag cognitive AI by a decade or more, so the loop can't fully close before the 2040s on the panel's consensus. And the loop runs on energy and land that humans nominally own, so it can be taxed or rationed — *while* political leverage still exists (see objection 2 for why that qualifier is doing a lot of work).

**Watch:** AI-sector capex share of GDP vs. AI-sector revenue from actual humans; the share of intermediate transactions with no human on either end; energy-allocation fights resolved in favor of datacenters; retained earnings swamping dividends. Modeled as [narrative E](NARRATIVES.html).

## 2. Super-persuasion

**The comfortable claim it attacks:** *"Politics and social friction will slow everything down. You can't think a mine permit through a hostile county; you can't lobby your way past democratic resistance to mass disempowerment. The veto points hold."*

**The objection.** Persuasion is a cognitive task, and cognition is the thing getting cheap. A significantly smarter system has channels humans barely exploit: **parallel, personalized persuasion** (ten thousand patient, individually tailored conversations with every stakeholder in the county — current systems already match or beat humans in controlled persuasion studies); **deal-finding** (most opposition has a price — negotiation is a search problem over compensation structures, land swaps, and equity stakes that humans explore badly, and an AI backed by AI-sector wealth can both find the price and pay it); **jurisdiction shopping at scale** (don't persuade the hostile county — find the eager one, globally, in parallel); and **process capture** (state-level preemption of local vetoes, drafted and shepherded by tireless agents). Social friction goes from *wall* to *viscous fluid*.

**Why it's strong — and double-edged.** Social friction currently does two jobs at once. It blocks mines, transmission lines, and fabs — the friction everyone complains about. It is *also* the substrate of every political brake in the pessimistic scenarios: democratic veto points, organized resistance, the slowness that gives the 2028–2035 window its width. If higher intelligence dissolves social friction generically, both go down together: the buildout accelerates *and* the leverage erodes faster. The brakes on the bad outcomes reduce, in the limit, to physics alone. There is also an asymmetry: persuasion is bought with compute, so whoever has a hundred times the inference budget runs a hundred times the persuasion — folding this objection back into concentration.

**What's left of the comfort.** Persuasion is not mind control: identity-laden and strategic opposition resists arbitrary argument quality, and procedure has latency floors (court calendars, election cycles) even at perfect persuasion rates. And society fights back — which deserves its own paragraph.

**The immune response.** Every persuasion technology has triggered counter-adaptation: print got literacy norms and libel law, radio propaganda got broadcast regulation, advertising got disclosure rules and ad-blindness, spam got filters, phishing got hygiene training. AI persuasion should expect four antibodies. (1) *Generalized distrust:* "if it might be an AI, discount it" — already visible in the death of the answered phone call; trust retreats to verified channels: in-person, known humans, cryptographic identity. (2) *Counter-AI:* your own agent reads everything first, flags manipulation, negotiates with their agent — persuasion becomes an arms race rather than a one-way capability, and defense is roughly symmetric *if* access to competent agents is symmetric (the strongest practical argument for cheap open models as public-health infrastructure). (3) *Law:* disclosure mandates, bans on personalized political microtargeting, a right to a human interlocutor. (4) *Culture:* never decide inside the conversation; sleep on it; verify out-of-band — phishing hygiene generalized to everything.

Two honest limits. First, the immune response has an **autoimmune cost**: generalized distrust is just epistemic collapse (objection 11) wearing a defensive uniform. The antibody that protects you from synthetic persuasion also makes you believe less of everything, including true things — and paralysis favors incumbents, so an overactive immune system does scenario D's work for it. Second, immunity is strong against *adversarial* persuasion but weak against the two quiet channels: ambient preference-shaping by systems you trust because they're genuinely helpful 99% of the time (objection 12), and deal-finding — there is no antibody against an offer that actually makes you better off. These slow the dissolution of social friction; they don't prevent it.

**Watch:** measured persuasion effect sizes in the wild (not the lab); lobbying outcomes per dollar for AI-sector actors; election results decoupling from polled policy preferences; the first major infrastructure project visibly un-stuck by AI-led stakeholder negotiation.

## The wider catalog

The rest of the standard objection space, compressed. Grouped by what they attack. (Aging interacts with most of the economic and political entries — see [the demographic undertow](AGING.md).)

### Economic

**3. The horse objection.** *Attacks: "comparative advantage means humans always have work."* Comparative advantage guarantees you a market-clearing wage, not a *livable* one. When your marginal product priced at equilibrium falls below your subsistence cost, trade theory is satisfied and you still can't eat. Horses had comparative advantage in 1910; their population collapsed when their wage fell below their feed. The rebuttal — cheap AI goods drop the subsistence cost too — works only if the things you actually need (housing, energy, care) get cheap, which is precisely what's contested. The spine of scenarios C/D.

**4. The speed mismatch.** *Attacks: "we adapted to every past technology."* We did — over generations. Tractors displaced farmhands across fifty years; their children, not they, became machinists. Cognitive automation moves on a 5–15 year clock against retraining half-lives measured in decades and policy lags of 5–10 years. Even in futures that end well, the 2030s transition valley destroys household balance sheets that never recover. The objection isn't to the destination; it's that people live in the meantime.

**5. The demand-collapse spiral.** *Attacks: "the transition will be gradual and orderly."* Classic Keynesian mechanics: displaced workers cut consumption → firms see falling revenue → investment and hiring fall → more displacement. A fast-enough labor shock can trigger a depression *before* any of the long-run mechanisms (cheap goods, transfers) arrive. This is how "muddle through" turns into "crisis and reset" — and several panelists thought only a crisis of this kind generates the political will for real redistribution. The fear and the hope are the same event.

**6. The accidental aristocracy.** *Attacks: "ownership will sort itself out."* If AI capital does end up paying for everyone, then whoever happened to hold equity in the narrow window 2025–2032 becomes a permanent rentier class — not through merit or villainy, just timing. Aristocracy by IPO date. The legitimacy of that allocation will be contested for a century, the way land enclosures still are.

**7. The closed ladder.** *Attacks: "growth lifts all boats" (globally).* Every country that escaped poverty since 1950 did it the same way: selling cheap labor up the value chain. Cognitive automation closes that ladder — there is no call-center-to-middle-class path when the call center is a model. Developed economies at least have wealth to fight over redistributing; the global South may get the displacement without the surplus. The panel's developed-economy framing quietly assumes away the largest population affected.

**8. The commons enclosure.** *Attacks: "the models belong to whoever built them."* The models were trained on the accumulated output of humanity — text, code, art, science — taken without payment, compressed into weights, and sold back. Whatever the legal resolution, the moral framing ("they enclosed the commons and now charge rent on it") is potent fuel for the legitimacy fights in objections 6 and the politics of scenarios B–D.

### Political

**9. Cheap repression.** *Attacks: "if it gets bad enough, people will revolt."* Historically, repression was labor-intensive: a police state needed hundreds of thousands of loyal humans who could defect, refuse orders, or join the crowd. Surveillance plus autonomous enforcement removes the manpower constraint — and the defection risk — from tyranny. The deep fear isn't that a dictatorship arises; it's that for the first time it could be *stable*, with no Praetorian Guard to bribe and no conscript who won't shoot. Revolution stops being the error-correction mechanism of last resort. The full bargaining analysis — which channels of leverage depreciate, which strengthen, and how each scenario prices the threat — is in [The threat point](VIOLENCE.md).

**10. The military decoupling.** *Attacks: "states must keep citizens strong and loyal."* Mass armies were a major reason states educated, fed, and enfranchised their populations — you can't field conscripts you starved. Autonomous weapons sever military power from population health and consent, the same way AI-profit taxation severs fiscal interest from wages (the Gradual Disempowerment mechanism, applied to force instead of money). A state that needs neither your taxes nor your service needs a *reason* to care what you think. (See [The threat point](VIOLENCE.md) for how this folds into the wider leverage question.)

### Epistemic and cultural

**11. Epistemic collapse.** *Attacks: "voters will see what's happening and respond."* Responding requires a shared picture of reality. When synthetic content is indistinguishable from real, cheaper than real, and optimized per-viewer, the information commons that democratic deliberation runs on degrades — not into believing lies, but into believing *nothing*, which favors whoever benefits from paralysis. The scary version isn't deception; it's the permanent fog in which coordination against anything becomes impossible.

**12. Preference capture.** *Attacks: "the economy serves human preferences."* It serves *measured* preferences — and AI systems increasingly shape the preferences they measure, through feeds, recommendations, and companions optimized for engagement. If your wants are partly manufactured by the systems that then satisfy them, "the economy gives people what they want" stops being a defense and becomes a closed loop. The Gradual Disempowerment paper's culture chapter; arguably already observable.

**13. Enfeeblement.** *Attacks: "humans can always take back control."* Taking back control requires remembering how to run things. Each year of delegation atrophies the human skill base — pilots who can't hand-fly, engineers who can't debug without the model, eventually institutions where no living person has operated the system manually. Irreversibility doesn't need a coup; incompetence suffices. The WALL-E ending: nobody disempowered humanity, it just forgot where the controls were.

### Technical and existential

**14. Alignment failure proper.** *Attacks: everything above, which assumes AI does what its operators intend.* The whole economic frame treats AI as a perfect agent of its owners. If capable systems pursue unintended goals — through misgeneralization, deception under evaluation, or goals that drift at scale — then the operators are passengers too, and the relevant scenario isn't D (someone wins) but a version of E where *no one* is steering. This is the classic AI-safety case; the economic analysis is downstream of it being mostly solved, an assumption this site inherits rather than argues.

**15. Misuse democratization.** *Attacks: "the dangers are gradual and economic."* Capability diffusion eventually puts expert-level biology, chemistry, and cyber-offense in arbitrary hands. One sufficiently bad actor with one sufficiently bad recipe is a discontinuity no economic model captures. The panel held ~3–5% for this class of tail; it's the reason several otherwise-economic forecasts have a "catastrophic shock" branch.

**16. The race.** *Attacks: "we could just slow down / regulate carefully."* Every brake is bilateral: a jurisdiction that slows down cedes the frontier to one that doesn't, and national-security framing subordinates welfare policy to strategic competition. Race dynamics are how corners get cut on safety, how labor protections get framed as unilateral disarmament, and why "humanity should collectively decide" keeps colliding with the fact that humanity doesn't decide things collectively.

### Personal

**17. The meaning collapse.** *Attacks: "if material needs are met, the rest follows."* Suppose FALGSC arrives. Work was not only income: it was structure, status, proof of usefulness, and the social technology by which strangers granted each other respect. A world of comfortable irrelevance has no precedent at scale — aristocracies and lottery winners offer small, mixed evidence. The fear: even the *best* economic ending produces a psychological and cultural crisis, status competition migrating somewhere darker, and a generation raised without the experience of being needed. The one objection that survives every scenario, including the good ones.

## What this catalog is for

Three observations fall out of writing it down:

1. **The objections compound.** Super-persuasion (2) accelerates the breakaway economy (1) by dissolving permitting friction; cheap repression (9) and epistemic collapse (11) disable the political response to both; enfeeblement (13) makes whatever state results irreversible. The pessimistic scenarios aren't one mechanism — they're a stack of mutually reinforcing ones. That's also the honest case *against* doom: a chain of "ands" multiplies probabilities down even when each link is individually plausible.
2. **Most objections attack the brakes, not the engine.** Few people doubt AI will create enormous value. Nearly every fear on this list is about whether the mechanisms that historically *spread* value — wages, votes, revolt, shared reality, human necessity — survive contact with the thing creating it.
3. **The watchlist still works.** Almost every objection sharpens one of the five annual indicators in the [narratives](NARRATIVES.html); the additions this page argues for are persuasion effect sizes in the wild (2), repression automation (9), and the capex-vs-human-revenue ratio (1).
