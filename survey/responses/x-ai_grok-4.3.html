1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)

  • S1 BROAD PROSPERITY (20%): Productivity gains diffuse through competitive markets and modest fiscal transfers, keeping median real consumption 30-60% higher than 2025 levels while labor income share stabilizes above 35%.
  • S2 MUDDLE THROUGH (40%): Automation displaces 35-50% of current task value but creates offsetting demand in coordination, maintenance, and new services; median household income stays within ±15% of today with higher variance and regional divergence.
  • S3 CONCENTRATION (25%): Top 0.1% capture 55-70% of AI-driven rents via compute and data ownership; median material living standards decline 10-20% after housing and energy costs, with voting influence diluted by campaign finance and regulatory capture.
  • S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT (10%): Labor share falls below 15%, states shift to AI-profit taxation, and institutions reorient toward infrastructure stability over citizen welfare, producing measurable drops in human policy influence even if GDP per capita rises.
  • S5 OTHER (5%): Regulatory or hardware bottlenecks stall frontier model progress for a decade, freezing automation at 2028-2030 capability levels.

2. Where Position A is right and where it fails

Position A correctly notes that past technologies raised absolute living standards even when capital owners captured large shares, and that a pure “robots owned by billionaires” model still leaves humans trading among themselves with today’s existing capital stock. Its strongest failure modes are (1) competition for scarce, non-replicable resources (urban land, energy sites, spectrum) where AI capital bids directly against human consumption, and (2) the capital threshold required to start a viable parallel human economy once the marginal cost of AI labor falls below subsistence wages, which rises sharply if compute and energy are already concentrated.

3. Where Position B is right and where it fails

Position B identifies the real risk that feedback loops weaken when tax revenue and military/economic power no longer require mass human participation; states funded by corporate profits have weaker incentives to maintain broad human welfare or political voice. Its strongest failure modes are (1) persistent elite demand for human loyalty and stability to reduce coup or unrest risk, and (2) historical evidence that even low-labor-share economies (e.g., Gulf states) still provide substantial citizen transfers and voice when a small native population holds formal sovereignty.

4. Key parameters

  • Fraction of tasks with >80% automation at <$30/hr equivalent (2025 dollars): currently ~25%; trajectory likely reaches 55-70% by 2035 if scaling continues.
  • Labor share of GDP: 58% today; most probable path is 35-42% by 2040, with faster drops if robotics hardware costs fall below $50k per human-equivalent unit.
  • Compute concentration (top 5 entities’ share of >10^27 FLOP training runs): ~70% today; likely stays above 60% absent antitrust, raising entry barriers for new human-controlled firms.
  • Price elasticity of demand for human-produced goods/services: low (-0.3 to -0.6) for status/luxury items, high (-1.5+) for routine goods, determining residual labor demand.
  • Effective redistribution rate (transfers + public services as % of AI rents): currently ~25% in OECD; probable range 15-35% depending on political coalitions.
  • Cost of subsistence-level robotic labor (food, shelter, maintenance): likely falls to $4-8k per year by 2035, below median human reservation wage in many regions.
  • Speed of capability diffusion to non-frontier actors (years from lab to <$1k devices): currently 3-5 years; compression to 1-2 years would accelerate displacement.

5. The single most compelling causal story

The median person’s 2046 welfare will be driven primarily by whether AI capital remains sufficiently diffuse that states continue to tax and spend on broad human consumption goods (housing, energy, healthcare) at rates above 20% of AI profits; when ownership concentrates above ~60% in five firms and those firms locate profits in low-tax jurisdictions, fiscal capacity for transfers collapses faster than new human-only niches can absorb displaced workers, producing a slow downward drift in real consumption and political weight even while aggregate output grows.
