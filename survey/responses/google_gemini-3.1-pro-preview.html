Here is an honest, calibrated assessment of the economic future under advanced AI, tailored to your context as a software engineer planning for the next two decades.

1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)

Subjective probabilities for developed economies, summing to 100%.

  • 20% - S1 BROAD PROSPERITY: Democratic institutions adapt quickly enough to implement robust redistribution (e.g., UBI, universal basic services), and the deflationary effects of AI make high-quality living standards accessible to the median person, who transitions to new forms of human-centric work or leisure.
  • 30% - S2 MUDDLE THROUGH: Institutional friction delays both catastrophic concentration and utopian redistribution; the median person experiences severe career volatility and stagnation in relative status, but absolute material conditions remain roughly at today's levels due to cheaper goods offsetting lost wages.
  • 20% - S3 CONCENTRATION: AI scales require immense capital, creating absolute monopolies where a tiny elite captures nearly all economic gains; democratic systems are captured by this wealth, leaving the median person structurally impoverished and reliant on inadequate, politically fragile welfare.
  • 20% - S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT: Position B plays out: GDP grows astronomically, but human cognitive and physical labor becomes obsolete, severing the feedback loops that force institutions to care about human flourishing, leading to a world optimized for machine-to-machine commerce where humans (including elites) are sidelined passengers.
  • 10% - S5 OTHER (Catastrophic/Stagnation): AI progress hits hard physical/data walls resulting in a plateau (stagnation), or advanced capabilities lead to catastrophic kinetic, biological, or infrastructural conflicts before a new economic equilibrium can settle.

2. Where Position A is right and where it fails

Steelman: Position A correctly applies the law of comparative advantage and the history of technological deflation. Even if an AI can do everything better than you, your labor still has a non-zero market clearing price. Furthermore, technology destroys jobs but creates wealth by drastically lowering the cost of goods. If AI makes housing, food, energy, and medicine 90% cheaper, your nominal wage can plummet while your actual standard of living rises. Humans will still desire human interaction, creating a massive service, entertainment, and artisanal economy.

Strongest failure modes: * Resource and Energy Competition: Position A assumes the "parallel human economy" can just hum along. But humans and AI both need electricity, land, and raw materials (copper, silicon, water). If AI generates massively higher returns per kilowatt-hour, it will outbid humans for physical resources, driving the baseline cost of survival out of reach for non-AI owners. * Capital Prerequisites: Humans cannot just "trade with each other" to build MRI machines or modern antibiotics. They require the frontier industrial base. If you don't have something the AI economy wants, you can't trade for these high-tech necessities. * Rent Extraction: The infrastructure of the future (compute, foundational models, robotics IP) will likely be highly monopolized. Humans may find themselves paying exorbitant rents to tech monopolies just to participate in the basic economy, draining whatever wealth the parallel human economy generates.

3. Where Position B is right and where it fails

Steelman: Position B correctly identifies that power is structural, not just malicious. Throughout history, states and economies have only cared about the masses because they needed them as factory workers, soldiers, and taxpayers. If AI and robotics replace these functions, the elite lose the structural incentive to invest in public education, public health, or infrastructure for the masses. Institutions will naturally optimize for the entities generating value (AI systems), slowly cutting humans out of the loop until democratic feedback mechanisms are entirely hollowed out.

Strongest failure modes: * Historical Resilience of Democracy: Voters still have votes, and the transition will take time. Before humans are completely economically disempowered, there will be a highly disruptive window where displaced middle-class voters (like software engineers) demand windfall taxes, UBI, or strict AI regulation. * Elite Self-Interest in Stability: Even a purely cynical, AI-owning elite needs physical security. A starving, desperate population of 8 billion people is a massive security threat. Keeping the masses comfortable with "bread and circuses" (which AI will make incredibly cheap to provide) is a rational security expense. * The "Human Premium": Position B underestimates human status-seeking. The wealthy will always pay a premium for things that are verifiably human precisely because they are inefficient and scarce—human-made art, human therapists, human waitstaff at luxury restaurants.

4. Key parameters

  1. Cost of Intelligence ($/FLOP or $/token): Determines how fast human cognitive labor is priced out. Trajectory: Exponentially decreasing. Likely to reach near-zero marginal cost for median SWE/knowledge tasks by the early 2030s.
  2. Compute Concentration (HHI of frontier models): Determines market power and whether we get a competitive market or an oligopoly. Trajectory: Consolidating rapidly. The $10B-$100B capital expenditures required for frontier clusters mean only 3-5 entities will control the foundation.
  3. Speed of Physical Automation (Robotics deployment rate): Determines when blue-collar labor joins white-collar labor in obsolescence. Trajectory: Lagging cognitive AI by 5-10 years due to physical world friction, but scaling laws are beginning to apply to robotics. Critical mass in the late 2030s.
  4. Energy Elasticity of AI (Price/kWh): Determines if AI starves humans of physical resources. Trajectory: Total energy demand will spike, causing localized grid strain and price hikes for consumers over the next decade, until next-gen nuclear/solar catches up in the 2040s.
  5. Redistribution Rate (% of AI GDP transferred to citizens): Determines material survival once wages drop. Trajectory: Will lag behind displacement. Expect a painful 5-10 year gap where unemployment rises before political gridlock breaks and robust UBI/transfers are implemented.
  6. Demand Elasticity for "Human-ness": Determines the size of the residual human labor market. Trajectory: High for luxury, zero for utility. People will pay for human politicians, pastors, and artists, but not for human coders, accountants, or drivers.

5. The single most compelling causal story

The most likely scenario for the median person in 2046 is a "Velvet Cage" (a hybrid of S2 and S4). The sheer deflationary force of AI will make basic digital and physical goods incredibly cheap, meaning you will not starve. The cost for the AI-oligopoly and the state to keep you housed, fed, and entertained via basic UBI and hyper-personalized digital media will be trivially low compared to total economic output. However, you will experience profound relative disempowerment. Upward mobility through labor will be largely dead. You will be materially comfortable but structurally irrelevant, dependent on a system you cannot influence, in an economy optimized for machine-to-machine interactions. It is not a dystopia of suffering, but a dystopia of lost agency.

6. Advice to this one person

As a software engineer facing this specific uncertainty, here are concrete actions to take:

  1. Front-load capital acquisition now: Your current SWE salary includes a premium that will likely evaporate in the next 5-10 years as AI coding agents scale. Live below your means now and convert your high labor income into capital. Invest in broad index funds, AI-adjacent monopolies, and hard assets (e.g., a paid-off primary residence). You want to cross the transition owning capital, not relying on selling your labor.
  2. Pivot from "writing code" to "orchestrating accountability": Stop defining yourself by your ability to write syntax. Move toward roles that require human trust, legal accountability, and physical-world integration. Systems engineering, regulatory compliance, defense tech, and technical sales/client management will remain human-in-the-loop much longer than pure software development, because corporations and governments require a human throat to choke when things go wrong.
  3. Build extreme local resilience: If global economic structures become alien or unresponsive (Position B), local physical networks become your safety net. Build high-trust relationships in your physical community. Live in a jurisdiction with a resilient local food/water supply, a stable local tax base, and low climate risk.
  4. Decouple your identity from your economic output: Software engineers are highly prone to tying their self-worth to their intelligence and productivity. If you are entering a world where a $10/month API is 100x smarter and faster than you, you must cultivate a philosophical, spiritual, or communal foundation for your life's meaning before the economic shock hits.
