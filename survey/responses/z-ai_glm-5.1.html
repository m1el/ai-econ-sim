z-ai/glm-5.1

1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)

  • S1 BROAD PROSPERITY (15%): AI drives massive productivity gains that diffuse through the economy via new job creation, wage growth, or functional redistribution (e.g., UBI), leaving the median person clearly better off.
  • S2 MUDDLE THROUGH (35%): AI causes significant cognitive labor displacement, but institutional friction, partial safety nets, and the creation of niche human-premium jobs keep the median person roughly where they are today, albeit in a more precarious and polarized labor market.
  • S3 CONCENTRATION (30%): The labor share of income crashes as cognitive automation outpaces new job creation; capital owners capture almost all surplus, and democratic institutions are hollowed out by elite dominance, leaving the median person materially worse off and politically powerless.
  • S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT (15%): Position B's trajectory plays out; high aggregate GDP masks the atrophy of human-leveraging feedback loops, as automated systems progressively run the economy and state while humans—even most elites—become vestigial to operational power.
  • S5 OTHER (5%): AI progress stalls due to data/compute/algorithmic walls, or catastrophic geopolitical/misuse events (e.g., bioterrorism, AI-enabled warfare) severely disrupts global economic baselines.

2. Where Position A is right and where it fails

Steelman: Position A correctly identifies that human wants are infinite, and a "post-labor" economy does not mathematically mandate starvation. If AI and robotics dramatically lower the cost of energy, food, and housing, the absolute floor of living standards rises. Furthermore, the "parallel human economy" argument holds water in a narrow sense: if a group of humans possesses capital and skills, they can trade with each other, and AI can act as an amplifier for their existing productivity. Historically, technological revolutions have never permanently reduced the labor share of income, because new tasks emerged faster than old ones were automated, and capital could not fully substitute for human adaptability.

Failure Modes: Position A fails by ignoring the difference between absolute material welfare and relative economic/political power. It assumes a frictionless parallel economy, but humans must compete for inelastic resources (land, urban proximity, clean water, energy grids) against AI-backed capital. If the wealthy no longer need human labor, they have no economic incentive to pay wages; the "parallel human economy" becomes a closed-loop system with severely depreciated capital, akin to a subsistence farm next to a hyper-advanced mega-factory. Furthermore, it assumes the state remains a neutral arbiter; but if tax revenue shifts from broad payroll taxes to narrow capital/AI profits, the state's constituency shifts accordingly, allowing capital owners to extract rents and enforce property rights that actively immiserate the parallel economy.

3. Where Position B is right and where it fails

Steelman: Position B correctly identifies the fundamental mechanism of democratic and institutional alignment: humans have historically mattered because their labor, cognition, and consumption were essential inputs to the economy and the state. Automation severs the dependency between capital and human welfare. As labor share drops to zero, the state no longer needs to educate, house, or keep healthy a workforce to maximize tax revenue; it merely needs to pacify them. Economic power naturally buys political power, and without the leverage of withholding labor, the masses lose their primary bargaining chip. The result is a structural, irreversible decoupling of institutional success from human flourishing, even if GDP skyrockets.

Failure Modes: Position B over-indexes on structural determinism and underestimates the resilience of democratic feedback loops that operate outside pure economic utility. Humans are not just economic inputs; they are physical agents capable of violence, social unrest, and ideological mobilization. States will still need to maintain monopoly on violence, requiring loyalty from military and police (who are human). Furthermore, elite self-interest in stability and the human psychological demand for human status goods, human leadership, and human-centric culture create persistent demand for human participation. It also assumes a monolithic "machine alternative," ignoring that AI systems might be highly competitive with each other, driving the price of cognition to zero and effectively democratizing cognitive labor before disempowerment can fully lock in.

4. Key parameters

  1. % of economically valuable tasks automatable at <2x median human cost (Trajectory: Rapid rise to 80%+ by 2046): This determines the ceiling of labor displacement. If AI remains expensive or narrow, human labor retains its premium; if cognitive and physical tasks become universally cheap, the labor share collapses.
  2. Elasticity of demand for human-specific goods/services (Trajectory: Moderate growth, hitting a ceiling): If demand for "human-made" or "human-interacted" goods expands infinitely as wealth grows, new jobs emerge. My guess is this elasticity is high at first (therapy, artisan goods, live entertainment) but ultimately finite and insufficient to absorb 50%+ of the workforce.
  3. Capital requirements for new market entrants (Trajectory: Initially low for software, eventually high for physical/robotic scale): If starting an AI-leveraged business remains cheap, wealth decentralizes; if dominating the economy requires massive compute/energy/robotic fleets, it heavily favors incumbents and accelerates concentration.
  4. Speed of physical robotics deployment (Trajectory: Lagging software by 5-10 years, scaling slowly due to supply chains): Software can displace cognitive workers instantly, but physical automation requires building things in the real world. The slower this is, the longer blue-collar and physical labor retains value, creating a bizarre split economy.
  5. Redistribution rate / Tax policy adaptability (Trajectory: Highly path-dependent, currently slow): The lag between labor displacement and institutional implementation of UBI/capital taxes. If this lag is >5 years, severe political instability and concentration occur before any safety net catches the falling labor share.
  6. Compute concentration (Trajectory: High and increasing): If frontier AI requires $100B+ training runs, the means of cognitive production are an oligopoly. If open-source/small models reach 90% of frontier utility, power decentralizes. I estimate compute remains highly concentrated due to energy and chip fabrication bottlenecks.
  7. Cost of energy per kWh (Trajectory: Slow decline, occasionally spiking): Energy is the ultimate substrate of both physical robotics and compute. If fusion/massive solar drives energy to near-zero, the surplus is vast and easier to redistribute; if it remains constrained, AI competes directly with humans for basic resource budgets.

5. The single most compelling causal story

The median person's welfare in 2046 will be determined by the decoupling of state revenue from human labor and the subsequent atrophy of the social contract. Historically, states maximized revenue by maximizing human productivity (via education, infrastructure, healthcare), creating a virtuous cycle where state and citizen interests aligned. As AI automates cognitive and physical labor, states will find it exponentially more efficient to tax the capital and compute of a few mega-corporations than to extract taxes from a fragmented, unemployed human populace. Once the state no longer requires a healthy, educated workforce to fund itself, the political incentive to maintain public goods, labor rights, and democratic representation evaporates. The median person will not be starved—basic caloric subsistence will be trivially cheap—but they will become economically superfluous and politically vestigial, surviving on depreciating, politically contingent transfers while institutional power shifts entirely to those who control the automated production pipelines.

6. Advice to this one person

  1. Shift from "writing code" to "owning the compute/capital stack": Your current leverage is cognitive labor, which is directly in the blast zone of AI. Aggressively convert a portion of your salary into equity in AI infrastructure (semiconductors, data centers, energy grid operators) rather than just SaaS companies, to ensure you own a slice of the capital that will replace you.
  2. Develop physical, localized, and high-friction skills: Software is infinitely replicable; the physical world is not. Invest time in skills that require physical presence, manual dexterity, or navigating local regulatory/human complexities (e.g., emergency medicine, specialized trades, local infrastructure management). These are harder to automate and provide a fallback labor value.
  3. Build a "parallel economy" network now: Position A's parallel economy only works if humans have capital and trust to trade with each other. Cultivate a robust network of people who own physical assets (land, housing, local businesses) and have practical, non-corporate survival skills. If institutional safety nets fail, your local mutual-aid network is your real social contract.
  4. Geographic arbitrage based on state capacity and violence monopolies: If states no longer need human labor, the quality of your local government's monopoly on violence becomes paramount. Move to or establish roots in jurisdictions with deep democratic traditions, high social cohesion, and localized resource independence (water, food, energy). Avoid jurisdictions where the state relies purely on resource extraction (which AI can take over) rather than human taxation.
  5. Avoid lifestyle creep to maximize optionality: The most dangerous scenario is being locked into a high-cost lifestyle that requires peak software-engineering wages to sustain. Keep your fixed costs low enough that you could survive on a fraction of your current income, giving you the flexibility to pivot, retrain, or step out of the corporate rat race before you are pushed out.
