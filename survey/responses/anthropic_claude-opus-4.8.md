# anthropic/claude-opus-4.8

## 1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)

- **S1 BROAD PROSPERITY — 20%**: Past general-purpose technologies did broadly raise living standards eventually; if diffusion is gradual and redistribution institutions adapt (even clumsily), median welfare can rise. But "eventually" did a lot of work historically and the labor-substitution dynamics this time are structurally different.
- **S2 MUDDLE THROUGH — 33%**: My modal outcome — partial automation, lumpy displacement, reactive policy (some UBI-like transfers, sectoral protections), median person materially similar but with thinner bargaining power and more income volatility. This is just "the last 40 years of wage stagnation amid GDP growth, accelerated."
- **S3 CONCENTRATION — 22%**: Capital and compute ownership are already concentrated; if labor's bargaining position collapses faster than political countervailing power organizes, you get rising transfers that are politically contingent and inadequate, plus weakened democratic leverage.
- **S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT — 17%**: Position B's mechanism is real and underrated, but I think full irreversibility within 20 years is less likely than partial versions of it that overlap with S3; I'm separating "humans lose effective steering" from "humans get poorer."
- **S5 OTHER — 8%**: Includes AI progress plateauing below the displacement threshold (~3%), catastrophic misuse/war/cyber (~3%), and genuine governed abundance (~2%).

(Sums to 100%. S3 and S4 overlap conceptually; I've split them by whether the binding problem is *distribution* vs. *control*.)

## 2. Where Position A is right and where it fails

**Steelman:** Metzger is correct that "the rich won't need us, so we starve" is economically illiterate as stated. Comparative advantage means trade persists even when one party is absolutely better at everything — humans can keep producing and trading among themselves with their existing capital and land. Productivity-enhancing tools have historically raised real wages, not destroyed them, and predictions of technological unemployment have a ~200-year losing streak. He's also right that "the 99%" already own substantial aggregate capital (homes, pensions, equity).

**Where it fails:**
- **Comparative advantage requires positive real wages, not just relative advantage.** If your marginal product, valued at the equilibrium price, falls below subsistence cost, comparative advantage doesn't save you — it just means you trade at a wage below what you need to live. Horses had comparative advantage too; their population collapsed when their wage fell below feed cost.
- **The "parallel human economy" needs capital and resources at competitive prices.** If AI/robotics bid up energy, land, compute, and materials, the human-only economy faces rising input costs while producing lower-value output. Subsistence is not guaranteed by current capital if that capital depreciates and replacements are priced for the AI economy.
- **Rent extraction and asset prices.** Aggregate household capital is real but unevenly held; if returns flow to capital not labor, the bottom 50% (who hold ~2% of US wealth) have little to ride the wave on.
- **Past-technology induction is weak** when the technology substitutes for *general cognition* rather than specific muscle/task. The reference class may be wrong.

## 3. Where Position B is right and where it fails

**Steelman:** This is the deeper argument. Institutions track human interests largely because they're *forced to* — states need conscripts and taxpayers, firms need workers and consumers, culture needs human attention. These are incentive structures, not benevolence. Sever the dependence (AI soldiers, AI-profit tax base, AI-generated culture optimizing engagement) and the feedback loops that auto-correct toward human welfare degrade. Crucially, this needs **no villain and no coordinated takeover** — it's an emergent equilibrium shift, which makes it hard to see, organize against, or reverse. The labor-share decline is already observable (US labor share fell from ~64% to ~57% since 2000).

**Where it fails:**
- **Consumer demand is a feedback loop too.** An economy of AIs producing for AIs with no human consumers is incoherent; someone must demand output. If humans hold purchasing power (via assets or transfers), firms remain incentivized to serve them.
- **Elite self-interest in stability.** Even self-interested elites generally prefer a stable, legitimate order to revolution; historically this has motivated redistribution (Bismarck, the New Deal). Disempowered-but-comfortable is a possible attractor.
- **Democratic resilience is underrated.** Voting, courts, and norms have survived prior concentration shocks. The paper somewhat assumes feedback loops are *only* economic; political identity and coalition dynamics have their own momentum.
- **Human demand for human-made/human-status goods** may stay robust (live sports, artisanal labels, personal services, status markets) — preserving a labor niche.
- **It under-weights heterogeneity and timing** — "irreversible" within 20 years is a strong claim.

## 4. Key parameters

1. **Share of economically valuable tasks automatable below human wage cost.** *The* master variable. If it crosses ~60–70% of hours within 15 years, labor bargaining collapses. Trajectory: rising fast in cognitive/digital work, slower in physical (robotics lags software by ~5–10 years).
2. **Capital/compute concentration.** Determines whether gains are shareable. Currently high and rising (frontier training compute concentrated in ~5 firms). Likely to stay concentrated absent intervention.
3. **Speed of diffusion vs. speed of policy adaptation.** Muddle-through requires policy to roughly keep pace. Diffusion looks faster than legislative cycles (~2–4 yr lag minimum). Bad sign.
4. **Redistribution rate / fiscal mechanism.** Whether AI rents get taxed and recycled. Currently near-zero design effort; politically hard once power has already shifted. Wide variance by country (Nordic > US).
5. **Elasticity of demand for human-produced goods/services.** If high (people genuinely prefer human-made), preserves a wage floor. Plausibly modest but nonzero — I'd guess 10–20% of consumption stays "human-premium."
6. **Cost trajectory of physical robotics + energy.** Determines whether the *physical* economy is captured or stays human. Energy especially: cheap energy accelerates everything. Falling but uncertain; robotics hardware still bottlenecked.
7. **Returns to capital vs. labor (capital share dynamics).** Already trending toward capital; the slope over 2026–2040 basically *is* the median-welfare question.
8. **Degree of human-in-the-loop legal/political requirement.** Regulatory mandates that humans must own/decide things create artificial demand for human participation. Highly contingent on near-term legislation.

## 5. The single most compelling causal story

The median person's 2046 welfare will most likely be determined by a **race between the collapse of labor's bargaining power and the construction of non-labor income channels (asset ownership + redistribution)** — and by default the collapse moves faster than the construction. Wages have historically been the mechanism that *automatically* couples productivity gains to mass welfare, requiring no political will. Once that automatic coupling weakens, mass welfare becomes a *political choice* that must be actively made and defended — and political choices about distribution are exactly what concentrated economic power tends to capture. So the most likely path isn't dramatic starvation (consumer-demand feedback and elite stability-preference prevent that) nor broad prosperity (no automatic mechanism delivers it); it's **a precarious, transfer-dependent median with eroded leverage** — S2 sliding toward S3 — where you're materially okay but structurally powerless, because the thing that made you economically necessary went away faster than anyone built a replacement for why society should care about you.

## 6. Advice to this one person

1. **Optimize for the transition decade, not the endpoint.** The highest-leverage software work for the next ~5–10 years is *building, deploying, and integrating AI systems*, not competing with them on raw coding. Become the person who makes AI useful inside organizations (evals, orchestration, domain-specific deployment, security). This is where wages stay high *longest*, and it buys you capital-accumulation time.

2. **Convert labor income into capital aggressively, now.** This is the most important concrete move and it directly hedges the S3/S4 risk. If labor share trends down and capital share up, you want to be on the capital side of the ledger. Maximize savings rate, hold broad equity exposure (you want to own the robots/compute, not race them), and treat your current high salary as a depreciating asset to be converted while it lasts.

3. **Build skills with a structural human premium.** Things that are valuable *because* a human does them or *because* you're accountable: senior technical judgment, trust relationships, regulated/licensed functions, physical-world integration, anything where liability or human accountability is legally required. Pure remote text-output work is the most exposed category.

4. **Acquire location and political optionality.** Redistribution rate (parameter 4) varies enormously by jurisdiction. Where you're a citizen/resident in 2035 may matter more for your welfare than your skills. Keep options open, and — non-trivially — engage politically on AI taxation/distribution *now*, while individual voices still have leverage, because per my section 5 that's the window that's closing.

5. **Don't fully insure against the disaster case — it's mostly uninsurable, and the muddle case is more likely than your prior suggests.** Your "concentration + suffering" prior is reasonable but I'd put it around 20–25%, not 60%. Plan primarily for S2 (precarious-but-okay): minimize fixed obligations (debt, mortgages calibrated to a salary that may not persist), keep liquidity, and value flexibility over status. The worst preparation is locking in a high-burn lifestyle that assumes today's software salaries continue linearly.
