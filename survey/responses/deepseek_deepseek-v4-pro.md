## 1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)
Subjective probabilities for developed economies, summing to 100%:

- **S1 BROAD PROSPERITY (15%)** — AI remains a tool-like multiplier that competitive markets and competent governance diffuse into broadly higher real incomes, and redistribution (e.g. UBI funded by AI taxes) fills gaps without destroying dynamism.
- **S2 MUDDLE THROUGH (30%)** — Automation displaces many jobs, but new human-centric services, political resistance, and patchy welfare keep the median person’s material standard roughly static, albeit with much higher precarity and inequality.
- **S3 CONCENTRATION (25%)** — Capital captures almost all productivity gains, median real incomes fall significantly, and concentrated wealth translates directly into hollowed-out democracy and inadequate, means-tested transfers that leave most people worse off.
- **S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT (25%)** — As described in Position B, human labour and cognition are progressively replaced, breaking the feedback loops that made institutions serve humans; GDP may stay high but human agency, even among elites, irreversibly erodes.
- **S5 OTHER (5%)** — Covers tail risks: AI capability growth plateaus far below AGI (energy/data/power limits), catastrophic misuse (bioweapons, war), or an unlikely “positive singularity” with robust global governance that delivers near-utopia.

*S3 and S4 are not mutually exclusive and could blend, but I separate them to distinguish material immiseration from loss-of-control dynamics.*

## 2. Where Position A is right and where it fails
**Steelman of Position A:**  
Metzger’s core insight is that previous labour-saving technologies never made the majority destitute because displaced workers found new ways to trade their remaining skills and capital. Even if a few billionaires own all the robots, they must sell their output to *someone* to earn a return; if the masses have no income, demand collapses and robot-owners go bankrupt. Therefore, either jobs reappear at high wages, or robot-owners *voluntarily* fund massive redistribution to maintain a consumer base. Meanwhile, the 99% still own their own human capital and existing physical capital—they can keep trading among themselves, and their absolute productivity doesn’t fall. So the floor is “no worse than today,” and the ceiling is vastly higher.

**Where it fails (strongest failure modes):**

1. **Land, energy, and resource inelasticity.** If AI+robotics supercharge output but primary resources (land, energy extraction rights, rare minerals, bandwidth) remain rivalrous and supply-inelastic, their owners can extract all the surplus via rents, leaving even a parallel human economy with no cheap base for subsistence.
2. **Positional goods and zero-sum competition.** A pure “everyone is richer” story collapses when relative status, political influence, and access to scarce amenities (coastal land, elite education, legal privileges) become the main currencies. Robot-owners don’t need a billion poor consumers; they need a few thousand wealthy peers and security forces.
3. **Capital barriers to the “parallel human economy.”** If production becomes super-capital-intensive, the 99%’s existing capital (houses, small businesses, human skills) may be worth almost nothing in AI-era purchasing power. Starting a competitive human-facing bakery requires zero human labour but might require an AI robot that costs $100,000—unattainable for the displaced.
4. **The consumer-demand feedback loop is breakable.** Owners can sell to other rich humans, to each other’s AI-run ventures, or to the state (which they control). The mass consumer base is not necessary if the elite’s consumption is what drives the accounting profits, because they own the entire supply chain and can consume directly without the circular flow depending on median household income.
5. **State capacity and elite incentives.** Even if redistribution would be economically rational, wealthy elites can capture the state and dismantle transfer programs, using AI-driven persuasion and surveillance to suppress unrest. A “no worse than today” floor assumes the pre-AI social contract persists, but the whole point is that it may not.

## 3. Where Position B is right and where it fails
**Steelman of Position B:**  
The gradual disempowerment thesis correctly identifies that modern institutions—labour markets, democratic states, cultural norms—evolved because societies *needed* broad human participation. When AI can outcompete humans in most economically and politically valuable roles, those feedback loops snap. Labour share trends toward zero, states funded by robot-taxation on a few large firms no longer depend on a healthy workforce, and economic power translates seamlessly into ownership of media, science, and political parties. The process is gradual, does not require a single coup, and can produce a world with high GDP growth where humans (including most current elites) find themselves irrelevant—their welfare irrelevant to the systems that keep running.

**Where it fails (strongest failure modes):**

1. **Democratic resilience and violent feedback.** Historical elites always wanted to fully disempower the masses but were repeatedly checked by revolution, labour movements, and the simple threat of societal breakdown. Advanced AI might make repression cheaper, but it might also enable mass mobilization and transparency. Elites have a strong self-interest in avoiding the chaos of billions of desperate, collapsed consumers.
2. **Human preference for human-made goods and “authentic” services.** There is robust evidence that people value goods with a human touch, from artisan food to live music to therapy. If demand for these is highly inelastic, a substantial human labour market persists even when machine alternatives are cheaper.
3. **Elite dependence on social and institutional stability.** Even a super-rich class cannot thrive without functioning legal systems, protection from pandemics, and some social peace. These public goods require a minimum level of broad prosperity and consent, providing a bargaining chip for the rest of society.
4. **AI is not a monolith; it may require humans in the loop longer than optimists think.** Alignment, liability, and regulation could mandate human sign-offs for many high-stakes decisions, preserving a professional class of human “human-in-the-loop” workers.
5. **Disempowerment is reversible before it is complete.** Position B may overstate the irreversibility: if the process takes decades, affected humans can organize, build parallel institutions, or use political tools (e.g. data strikes, voting blocs) to reclaim influence before the automation of state violence is total.

## 4. Key parameters
These are the quantitative levers that most determine which scenario unfolds. Rough guesses are for 2040-2046, conditional on no catastrophic stall.

| Parameter | Why it matters | Rough guess & trajectory |
|-----------|----------------|--------------------------|
| **Fraction of economically valuable tasks automatable at below-human cost** | Defines the size of the labour displacement shock and the base for human bargaining power. | 50-70% of 2025 task-weight by 2035; 70-90% by 2045. Broad occupations, not just physical. |
| **Capital share of gross income (pre-tax, pre-transfer)** | Tracks the shift from labour earnings to asset ownership. If it rises from ~30% to >60%, the majority lose their primary income source. | Likely >50% by 2040 in most high-income economies if no aggressive policy intervention. |
| **Elasticity of demand for human-produced goods/services** | Determines whether a “quaint human economy” can absorb many workers at decent wages, or whether preference is swamped by cost differences. | Modest (0.3-0.6) for generic goods; higher (0.8-1.5) for care, arts, elite services, but overall weighted average probably <0.5, insufficient. |
| **Cost of subsistence-enabling robotics/AI (e.g., robot that can build a basic house, grow food)** | If basic goods become nearly free through automation, absolute poverty could vanish even without redistribution, making concentration less materially painful. | Automation can drive marginal cost of many goods near zero, but land, permitting, and energy remain bottlenecks; true all-inclusive subsistence cost might drop 50-70% but not to zero. |
| **Rate of redistribution (effective transfer size as % of GDP, universality)** | Determines the floor for the median person if labour income shrinks. | Without a crisis, 5-15% of GDP for UBI-style transfers by 2040; with sustained unrest, could rise to 20-30%, but political feasibility low in US/EU without major realignment. |
| **Compute concentration (top-3 actors’ share of total effective compute)** | Affects whether AI capability is oligopolistic. High concentration enables rent extraction and political capture; low concentration (open-source) could diffuse ownership. | Likely highly concentrated (top 3 > 70%) driven by capital, data, and specialized engineering, barring open-weight breakthroughs. |
| **Speed of diffusion vs. retraining/adaptation hysteresis** | If displacement is faster than human retraining and institutional churn, transitional immiseration is severe, triggering political cascades. | Diffusion likely in 5-15 year waves, faster than any prior technology; human reskilling half-lives measured in decades; gap > 10 years is plausible. |
| **Energy abundance and access cost** | Energy is the ultimate physical input; cheap abundant energy (via solar, fusion, AI-optimized grids) makes material abundance easier, while constraints worsen zero-sum dynamics. | Real cost of electricity could fall ~30-60% by 2045, but transmission, storage, and regulatory capture may limit pass-through to final users. |

## 5. The single most compelling causal story
The median person’s welfare in 2046 will be overwhelmingly determined by whether the decline in mass labour demand triggers a **positive-feedback loop of political disenfranchisement that pre-empts redistribution**, or whether countervailing forces (elite fear of instability, mass movements, or globally competitive tax regimes) manage to **institutionalize a broad claim on AI-derived income before the old social contract fully unravels**. If labour loses its economic function before laws lock in meaningfully high universal transfers or citizen-equity stakes, political power will follow the money: the few who own the compute will capture legislative, judicial, and cultural authority, dismantle safety nets, and use automated enforcement to suppress dissent, locking in a low-welfare equilibrium for the majority—even if GDP per capita explodes. Historically, every major expansion of the franchise and welfare state happened because the ruling class needed soldiers, workers, or consumers. In a world where they need none of those, the irreversibility of political capture becomes the master variable, and it will be decided in the short window—likely 2028-2035—when the labour share begins a sharp decline but mass democratic participation still retains some power.

## 6. Advice to this one person
As a software engineer already aware of the risks, these actions hedge across the scenarios without requiring perfect foresight:

1. **Shift income mix toward non-labour claims on AI productivity.** Build a stake in widely diversified equity (especially firms likely to benefit from AI capital deepening, including energy, land, and compute owners), acquire rental real estate that could serve as a supply-constrained asset, and explore royalties or small-platform ownership rather than pure hourly coding. Aim for >30% of net wealth in such assets within 5-7 years.

2. **Cultivate “human-moat” skills that resist both automation and offshoring, but in a lucrative niche.** High-trust advising (e.g. personal wealth management for mid-sized clients, estate planning, psychotherapy-adjacent coaching), on-site physical/diagnostic trades (electrician, medical technician with AI tools), or political advocacy/regulatory strategy where personal credibility and elite network matter. These are roles where demand for human presence is inelastic and not easily substituted by a chatbot.

3. **Geographically hedge toward communities with high social cohesion and local resilience.** Even in a concentration scenario, towns with strong agricultural hinterlands, mutual aid networks, and local democratic institutions can buffer state-level dysfunction. Consider living in or spending significant time in such a place within the next 5 years, building ties and acquiring practical self-sufficiency skills (food, energy, security).

4. **Engage collectively and early—irreversibility is the enemy.** Join or fund organisations pushing for concrete pro-labour AI policy (public AI infrastructure, data dividends, sovereign wealth funds that invest in automation and pay dividends to every citizen). The window to embed broad-based claims is narrow; individual career moves won’t be enough if the political lock-in described in Section 5 solidifies.

5. **Maintain a margin of safety that works in both inflation and deflation.** Save enough liquid, globally diversified assets—a mix of inflation-protected bonds, physical gold or bitcoin (if you accept crypto), and essential-use commodities—to survive a prolonged income disruption. Do not assume existing social safety nets will remain intact; plan for a 2-3 year runway without any government support, starting now.
