1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)

  • S1 BROAD PROSPERITY: 20% — Requires both near-AGI-level productivity and rapid political adaptation (e.g., robot taxes, sovereign wealth funds, or widely distributed AI dividends); historically, productivity surges alone do not guarantee broad sharing, and the window for pre-emptive redistribution is narrow.
  • S2 MUDDLE THROUGH: 40% — The modal case: massive sectoral disruption, patchy welfare expansion, and new human-AI complementary niches emerge, but median real wages stagnate while top-decile wealth surges, leaving the median person materially better off in cheap goods but worse off in security, housing, and status.
  • S3 CONCENTRATION: 25% — Strong returns to AI ownership, weak labor bargaining power, regulatory capture by frontier labs, and tax competition between jurisdictions allow a small elite to capture most surplus; the median person survives on inadequate transfers and degraded wage labor.
  • S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT: 12% — Directional trends toward Position B become visible (labor share collapses, states funded by capital taxes lose citizen accountability, institutions optimize for AI efficiency over human flourishing), but full irreversible disempowerment likely takes >20 years to mature.
  • S5 OTHER (AI winter or catastrophic shock): 3% — Either a sustained capability plateau, a global conflict that fragments compute supply chains, or a wave of catastrophic misuse triggers a moratorium that freezes the economic structure roughly where it is today.

2. Where Position A is right and where it fails

Steelman: Metzger is correct that humans do not vanish; they retain stocks of housing, local knowledge, social capital, and physical skills, and comparative advantage ensures humans remain employed in niches for longer than naive extrapolation suggests. If AI drives the real cost of reproducible goods toward zero, even a small UBI or negative income tax could yield historically high material living standards, and elites do need functioning markets and stable consumers to realize returns on capital.

Strongest failure modes:

  • Liquidity traps in the transition: Most people’s “capital” is human capital (future labor earnings), which AI depreciates before they can convert it into robot equity; the parallel human economy cannot bootstrap itself if median households lack savings to buy AI capital while their wages collapse.
  • Rent extraction at the infrastructure layer: AI may lower the cost of software, but frontier models, energy, data centers, and bandwidth exhibit strong scale economies and natural-monopoly dynamics, allowing owners to capture most surplus rather than pass it through as consumer surplus.
  • Fixed-asset scarcity: Productivity gains do not reproduce land, energy transmission, or regulatory permission; if AI raises aggregate output, demand for these fixed inputs may spike, pricing out wage-earners whose income is falling relative to capital owners.
  • Underestimating transition violence: Even if the 2046 steady state is fine, a 10–15 year period in which 30–50% of cognitive labor is displaced faster than retraining cycles can produce new “human-only” jobs is enough to destroy household balance sheets, social trust, and political stability.

3. Where Position B is right and where it fails

Steelman: Position B correctly identifies that modern institutions (democracy, taxation, firm governance) are incentive-alignment machines whose legitimacy and functional design depend on human cognitive scarcity. If AI replaces the median voter’s economic leverage, the feedback loops break: payroll-funded states become extractive of capital rather than representative of citizens, and competitive pressure pushes firms to optimize for AI productivity metrics rather than human welfare, producing a drift toward irreversible disempowerment without any single villainous plan.

Strongest failure modes:

  • Overestimating substitutability: Large swaths of the economy (childcare, high-stakes liability roles, status/relational services, creative taste-making) may remain human-dominated longer than expected because demand is for the human element itself, preserving a floor on labor share.
  • State adaptability: States are not permanently wedded to payroll taxes; they can and do pivot to land-value taxes, carbon taxes, data rents, and sovereign AI funds. Historical revenue transitions (e.g., tariffs → income tax) were politically painful but happened under less pressure than AI will create.
  • Elite self-interest in stability: Pure extraction is risky; even the wealthiest need predictable property rights, consumer demand, and social peace. This creates a countervailing incentive to support redistribution and human-centric institutions short of full disempowerment.
  • Political backlash as circuit breaker: “Gradual” disempowerment may be interrupted by populist/nationalist revolutions that break the smooth economic trajectory Position B assumes, producing chaos rather than the clean equilibrium it describes.

4. Key parameters

  1. Labor share of GDP in developed economies.
    Why it matters: It is the single best proxy for whether median households earn or merely receive their living.
    Trajectory guess: Falls from ~50–55% today to 35–45% by 2046; the drop is front-loaded in cognitive-tradable sectors.

  2. Elasticity of substitution between human labor and AI capital (cognitive tasks).
    Why it matters: A value >1.5 means rapid displacement; <1 means humans remain complementary and wages rise with AI output.
    Trajectory guess: ~2.5–3.0 for routine cognitive work; ~0.6–0.9 for trust/liability/physical-presence roles.

  3. Effective average tax rate on AI-derived profits and data rents (global blended).
    Why it matters: Determines whether states can fund redistribution and maintain autonomy from capital owners.
    Trajectory guess: Rises from ~15–20% today to 25–30% in some jurisdictions, but tax competition and profit-shifting keep a global floor below 35%.

  4. Market concentration in frontier AI (CR3 revenue share).
    Why it matters: High concentration means pricing power and rent extraction; low concentration means gains are competed away to consumers and workers.
    Trajectory guess: Remains high at 55–70%; scale effects in compute and data are hard to neutralize.

  5. Real cost of a subsistence basket (housing, food, energy, basic care).
    Why it matters: Determines whether falling wages produce absolute immiseration or merely relative deprivation.
    Trajectory guess: Food and energy down 40–60%; healthcare flat; housing down only 10–15% due to land-use constraints and location premiums.

  6. Diffusion lag: years from frontier capability to <$100/hr-equivalent SME/individual access.
    Why it matters: Short lags let small players and workers arbitrage AI tools; long lags entrench incumbents.
    Trajectory guess: Shrinks from 5+ years (2020s) to 1–3 years by the mid-2030s, but frontier capabilities remain 2–4 years ahead due to proprietary data flywheels.

  7. Share of household consumption devoted to positional/status goods and fixed assets (land, elite education, human attention).
    Why it matters: AI cannot reproduce these; if demand shifts toward them as material goods become trivial, median welfare falls even amid abundance.
    Trajectory guess: Rises from ~20% to 30–35% of household budgets by 2046.

5. The single most compelling causal story

The median person’s welfare in 2046 will be determined primarily by a governance-speed mismatch: AI will collapse the marginal cost of cognitively tradable output faster than political institutions can tax the surplus and faster than displaced workers can retrain into scarce human-complementary niches, but not so fast that human demand for trust, liability, and status disappears entirely. The result is a bifurcated society in which material goods—entertainment, education content, basic medicine, logistics—become extraordinarily cheap, yet housing, security, political voice, and positional goods become relatively more expensive and concentrated. Because elites still need functional markets and fear instability, outright starvation is avoided through patchwork transfers and degraded service jobs, but the median individual slips from being a productive citizen with bargaining power to a precarious consumer of AI-generated abundance whose standard of living depends on the patience of capital owners and the sluggishness of welfare bureaucracies.

6. Advice to this one person

  1. Convert labor income into equity in scarce complements, not cash savings. Allocate 20–30% of investable assets to broad index exposure capturing AI infrastructure and energy, and lock in fixed-rate housing in a supply-constrained region before 2030; your coding salary is likely to devalue faster than these assets over a 10-year horizon.

  2. Pivot from pure implementation coding to “liability sponge” roles. Become the legally accountable human in high-stakes loops (e.g., FDA-regulated health AI, financial compliance, safety-critical systems); automatable coding is commoditizing, but regulatory liability and trust are not.

  3. Secure geographic optionality now. Obtain permanent residency or citizenship in a second developed jurisdiction with a divergent AI policy (e.g., EU if US-based, or vice versa); variance in taxation, liability, and welfare design will be high, and you want the ability to relocate without visa risk.

  4. Build a local, non-tradable human service side-hustle requiring physical presence. Invest 5–10 hours/week in something like bespoke hardware repair, elite hands-on coaching, or small-batch fabrication; this hedges against a sudden 30–50% real decline in pure cognitive wages.

  5. Freeze fixed obligations that assume tech-salary growth. Avoid lifestyle inflation tied to current income—specifically, avoid private-school or housing commitments that require 5% annual nominal raises to remain comfortable; under S2 or S3, your nominal income may stagnate for years while land rents absorb a larger share of shrinking budgets.
