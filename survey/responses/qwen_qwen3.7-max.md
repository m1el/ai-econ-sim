## 1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)

- **S1 BROAD PROSPERITY (20%):** AI acts as a massive deflationary force on essential services (healthcare, education, software), and institutional adaptations (like robust UBI or negative income taxes) successfully distribute the immense surplus, leaving the median person with significantly higher real purchasing power.
- **S2 MUDDLE THROUGH (35%):** Technological displacement outpaces institutional adaptation, leading to a messy transition where digital goods become incredibly cheap but physical/positional goods inflate, leaving the median person with a similar overall standard of living but higher economic precarity and variance.
- **S3 CONCENTRATION (20%):** AI accelerates winner-take-all dynamics in capital markets; the owners of frontier models and compute capture the vast majority of the surplus, while the median person relies on stagnant wages or inadequate state transfers, resulting in severe wealth inequality and weakened democratic influence.
- **S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT (15%):** The structural leverage of human labor collapses, causing states and corporations to slowly decouple their decision-making from human welfare; even if material baseline needs are met via cheap AI goods, humans lose effective agency, political voice, and control over the trajectory of their own institutions.
- **S5 OTHER (10%):** AI progress hits a hard data/compute wall resulting in a prolonged "AI winter," or conversely, a rapid intelligence explosion leads to either utopian post-scarcity governance or catastrophic misalignment, bypassing the 20-year economic gradient entirely.

## 2. Where Position A is right and where it fails

**Where it is right:** Position A correctly identifies the lump of labor fallacy and the power of comparative advantage. Even if AI achieves absolute advantage in all cognitive tasks, humans will retain a comparative advantage in tasks where AI compute/energy is relatively more expensive than human time, or where human accountability is legally/socially mandated. Furthermore, Position A correctly anticipates the massive deflationary effect of AI: if the cost of software, media, legal analysis, and medical diagnostics drops by 99%, the real purchasing power of a stagnant nominal wage still rises significantly.

**Where it fails:** Position A assumes frictionless labor mobility and ignores the constraints of the physical world. First, it ignores **land, energy, and resource competition**: as AI drives massive economic growth, the wealth generated will bid up the price of physical land, raw materials, and energy, potentially eating all the deflationary gains in digital goods. Second, it assumes humans have the **capital requirements** to participate in the "parallel human economy"; if AI owners capture the surplus, ordinary humans lack the capital to invest in their own comparative advantage. Finally, it ignores **transition speed**: a 50-year-old software engineer cannot seamlessly pivot to an "AI-aligned human-centric artisan" in the two years it takes for their current job to be automated.

## 3. Where Position B is right and where it fails

**Where it is right:** Position B correctly identifies the "bargaining power" theory of institutions. Historically, democracy, labor rights, and the welfare state emerged largely because elites needed the masses for industrial labor and mass-military defense. If AI provides both cognitive labor and physical security (via drones and robotics), the structural leverage of the masses evaporates. Position B rightly points out that the feedback loop tying institutional alignment to human flourishing is contingent on human utility; once that utility drops, there is no historical precedent to guarantee that elites or automated systems will voluntarily maintain expensive redistributive and democratic structures.

**Where it fails:** Position B underestimates **institutional inertia, elite self-interest, and human demand for human goods**. First, democratic institutions have massive friction; voters will aggressively use the ballot box to demand redistribution (e.g., robot taxes, compute dividends) long before they are fully disempowered, creating a messy but effective counter-force. Second, elites still need a stable, functioning society to safely enjoy their wealth; a dystopian slum surrounding a gated compound is a security risk and psychologically unpleasant. Third, **status is inherently human**: people will pay massive premiums for human-made art, human care, and human leadership simply *because* it is human, creating a resilient economic niche that AI cannot fulfill by definition.

## 4. Key parameters

1. **Cost of cognitive automation vs. human wage (e.g., $/hour of AI work vs. median human wage):** Determines the speed and breadth of white-collar displacement; if AI drops below $2/hour for median cognitive tasks, disruption will be violent and rapid. *Trajectory: Dropping exponentially, likely crossing the median human wage threshold for many tasks within 5-7 years.*
2. **Elasticity of demand for AI-generated goods/services:** If high, AI creates massive new industries and absorbs displaced labor (Jevons paradox); if low, it simply replaces existing labor. *Trajectory: High for digital entertainment and software, but ultimately bounded by human time and attention.*
3. **Capital share of income (vs. Labor share):** Currently around 30-40% in developed economies; if it trends toward 60%+, the concentration scenario becomes mathematically inevitable without massive state intervention. *Trajectory: Slowly rising, likely to accelerate sharply once physical robotics become cost-effective.*
4. **Cost of general-purpose physical robotics:** The price point at which a humanoid robot becomes cheaper than minimum-wage human labor. This determines if blue-collar work is automated alongside white-collar work, removing the "physical world" comparative advantage. *Trajectory: Currently >$100k, likely to drop below $30k (the critical threshold) within 10-15 years.*
5. **State revenue composition (Payroll vs. Capital/Compute taxes):** The percentage of state revenue derived from taxing human labor versus taxing AI compute, corporate profits, or land. This determines if the state remains financially incentivized to keep human citizens employed and happy. *Trajectory: Currently >70% payroll/income; will be forced to shift, but political friction will delay this transition.*
6. **Speed of model diffusion (Years from frontier to commodity):** How fast open-source or smaller models catch up to the frontier. Fast diffusion democratizes the technology and prevents monopoly rents; slow diffusion concentrates power in a few mega-caps. *Trajectory: Currently 1-2 years behind frontier; likely to remain fast due to open-weight momentum and algorithmic efficiencies.*
7. **Energy and Compute Concentration (Gini coefficient of physical AI infrastructure):** How concentrated the physical data centers, power plants, and chip supply chains are. High concentration creates an insurmountable moat for incumbent tech giants. *Trajectory: Increasing in the short term due to massive CapEx requirements, but may decentralize long-term as hardware commoditizes.*

## 5. The single most compelling causal story

The median person's welfare in 2046 will be primarily determined by the macroeconomic divergence between the **hyper-deflation of digital/cognitive goods** and the **hyper-inflation of physical/positional goods**. AI will likely make software, media, education, and basic administrative services incredibly cheap, fulfilling Position A's promise of increased baseline utility. However, humans still live in the physical world, and the immense wealth generated by AI will aggressively bid up the price of physical land in desirable areas, energy, raw materials, and positional status. Therefore, the median person's welfare will not be determined by their ability to produce digital value (which will approach zero), but by their ownership of scarce physical assets and their political ability to secure a share of the AI-generated surplus to pay for increasingly expensive physical living costs.

## 6. Advice to this one person

1. **Shift from "writing code" to "owning systems" and "bridging atoms":** Pure software engineering is the most exposed sector to cognitive automation. Pivot your career toward the intersection of software and the physical world—such as robotics integration, energy grid optimization, biotech, or physical supply chains—where digital outputs must be validated against messy physical realities.
2. **Accumulate scarce physical and positional assets early:** Since AI will deflate digital goods but inflate physical ones, use your current high software engineering salary to buy physical assets that cannot be automated. Invest in real estate in resilient, high-quality-of-life areas (avoiding hyper-financialized tech hubs that might hollow out), and hold equities in energy, utilities, and raw materials.
3. **Build "high-trust" human networks and a reputation for execution:** In a world flooded with infinite AI-generated code, content, and analysis, trust, verification, and human accountability will become the ultimate scarce resources. Cultivate a personal brand and professional network based on reliability, real-world execution, and the ability to take legal/social liability for outcomes—things an LLM cannot do.
4. **Develop a "barbell" skill strategy:** Combine high-level AI orchestration (using AI agents to do 10x the technical work) with a deeply human, highly regulated, or physical skill. For example, pair your AI-augmented coding ability with local community organizing, physical engineering, or healthcare-adjacent technology, ensuring you have one foot in the hyper-productive digital world and one foot in the irreplaceable human/physical world.
