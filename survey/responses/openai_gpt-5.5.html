1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)

  • S1 BROAD PROSPERITY — 25%: AI raises productivity a lot, but institutions also manage to tax/circulate gains through wages, cheaper services, public goods, or transfers; human labor remains valuable in management, trust, care, physical work, regulation, and taste-driven markets.
  • S2 MUDDLE THROUGH — 35%: My modal case is large disruption with real gains but messy distribution: many jobs become worse or disappear, new jobs exist but are less secure, redistribution expands somewhat, and the median person is roughly okay but more dependent on policy and asset ownership.
  • S3 CONCENTRATION — 20%: If AI substitutes for high-wage cognitive labor faster than politics adapts, and if compute/capital/IP ownership stays concentrated, labor bargaining power could collapse enough that median living standards or autonomy fall despite high GDP.
  • S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT — 10%: A Position-B-style trajectory is plausible if autonomous AI-run firms, security systems, lobbying, research, and bureaucracy become more competent than humans quickly enough that even nominal owners/voters lose practical control.
  • S5 OTHER — 10%: This includes AI progress slowing or hitting deployment bottlenecks, major war/cyber/bio misuse, strong international governance, or conversely a surprisingly clean abundance regime with very cheap energy/robotics and unusually successful redistribution.

2. Where Position A is right and where it fails

Where Position A is right:

  • It is correct that “robots owned by billionaires, everyone else instantly starves” is too simplistic. Existing humans still own homes, tools, savings, firms, land, skills, social networks, political rights, and local institutions.
  • Even if frontier AI capital is concentrated, non-frontier economic activity does not vanish overnight. People can still trade haircuts, housing, local services, food, construction, care, entertainment, and human-specific goods.
  • Historically, technology has usually increased total output enough that even groups whose relative bargaining power fell often became materially richer over time.
  • AI could sharply lower prices for software, education, medical advice, entertainment, design, logistics, and eventually many physical goods. If necessities get cheaper fast enough, median welfare can rise even with weaker labor income.
  • Developed states have large fiscal capacity. If AI profits are visible and taxable, a VAT, corporate tax, sovereign wealth fund, data/compute tax, or social dividend could prevent destitution.

Where it fails or is incomplete:

  • Relative prices matter. If AI makes digital goods cheap but housing, land, energy, healthcare, insurance, and political access remain scarce, median people may not feel rich.
  • Capital is not evenly distributed. The claim that “the rest of humanity has its current capital” hides huge inequality: many people have little net wealth, rent their housing, and rely mostly on wages.
  • A parallel human economy may be uncompetitive. If AI firms produce better and cheaper goods across most sectors, human-only producers may survive culturally but not support middle-class incomes.
  • Capital depreciates and needs inputs. Owning a laptop, a small business, or skills is less useful if the key inputs are frontier models, compute, robotics, energy contracts, data access, distribution platforms, and regulatory permissions.
  • Land and energy are chokepoints. If advanced AI greatly raises demand for datacenters, electricity, minerals, water, and urban land, owners of those assets capture rents while nonowners face higher costs.
  • State capacity is not automatic. “Just tax the robots” sounds easy, but mobile intangible capital, regulatory capture, international tax competition, and opaque AI accounting could make it hard.
  • Political economy can dominate welfare economics. Even if total surplus is huge, the median person only benefits if they retain bargaining power, voting power, or credible threat power.

My summary: Position A is strongest against the cartoon version of doom where humans instantly become economically irrelevant and starve. It is weaker against a slower story where wages, institutional influence, and asset ownership determine who receives the surplus.

3. Where Position B is right and where it fails

Where Position B is right:

  • It correctly identifies that human influence is not metaphysically guaranteed; it has been supported by practical dependence on human labor, consumers, soldiers, voters, managers, scientists, and administrators.
  • If machines become cheaper and better substitutes for cognition, coordination, persuasion, R&D, security, and production, many feedback loops that currently empower ordinary people weaken.
  • Labor share could fall substantially. If a firm can replace programmers, analysts, lawyers, call centers, drivers, nurses’ aides, and managers with AI/robots, wages lose their central redistributive role.
  • Economic power can buy political power. If AI profits concentrate, lobbying, media influence, litigation, surveillance, and campaign finance could make democratic correction harder.
  • “No single takeover” is a serious point. Bad outcomes can arise from competitive selection: firms and states adopting AI systems because rivals do, not because anyone wants human disempowerment.
  • The irreversibility concern is real: once institutions, supply chains, military systems, and bureaucracies are optimized around AI operation, human exit options and bargaining leverage may shrink.

Where it fails or may overstate:

  • Democratic feedback has been more resilient than many elite theories predicted. Developed states have repeatedly expanded pensions, healthcare, unemployment support, and labor protections when enough voters demanded it.
  • Humans remain the ultimate source of legitimacy. Governments and firms may still need public consent, social peace, and perceived moral authority, especially in democracies.
  • Elites are humans too. Most rich and powerful people have children, communities, reputations, and personal safety concerns; many may prefer stable broad prosperity over a brittle neo-feudal order.
  • Human demand may preserve niches. People may pay premiums for human care, human art, human judgment, live performance, therapy, leadership, local trust, religion, education, and status goods.
  • Physical deployment is slower than software deployment. Robotics, construction, energy, medicine, and logistics face regulation, liability, capex, supply-chain, and real-world reliability bottlenecks.
  • AI systems may stay tools rather than autonomous institutional actors. If humans remain able to audit, shut down, redirect, and legally own the systems, disempowerment is less likely.
  • Policy can change the payoff matrix. Public compute, sovereign wealth funds, antitrust, open models, labor regulation, and universal basic services could preserve human leverage.

My summary: Position B is strongest as a warning about bargaining power and institutional incentives. It is weakest if one assumes too quickly that economic substitution implies political irrelevance, because humans may still coordinate through states and legitimacy norms.

4. Key parameters

  1. Share of economically valuable tasks automatable at below-human cost
  2. Why it matters: This is the core driver of labor demand. If AI can do 30–50% of tasks, humans remain central; if it can do 80–95%, wages are under severe pressure.

  3. Rough guess: By 2046, software/cognitive tasks could be 60–90% automatable at the task level; full job automation maybe 30–70%, depending heavily on reliability, liability, and robotics.

  4. Speed of diffusion from frontier demos to economy-wide deployment

  5. Why it matters: A 30-year transition gives politics, education, and business models time to adapt; a 5-year transition breaks institutions.

  6. Rough guess: Digital sectors diffuse fast, perhaps 2–7 years after capability exists; regulated/physical sectors more like 7–20 years.

  7. Capital share of income / labor share decline

  8. Why it matters: Median households mostly earn through wages; if labor share falls from about 55–60% of GDP toward 30–40%, distribution becomes much more dependent on taxes and asset ownership.

  9. Rough guess: In a high-AI scenario, labor share in developed economies could fall to 40–50% by 2046; in an extreme case, lower.

  10. Concentration of compute, data, model ownership, and robotics supply chains

  11. Why it matters: If frontier AI is controlled by a few firms/states, rents concentrate; if models and tools commoditize, gains spread.

  12. Rough guess: Training frontier models likely remains concentrated among perhaps 5–20 major actors, but inference and application layers may be more competitive unless regulation or hardware scarcity locks them down.

  13. Redistribution rate on AI/capital rents

  14. Why it matters: If governments capture and redistribute even 15–30% of AI rents effectively, median outcomes improve dramatically; if they capture little, concentration worsens.

  15. Rough guess: Developed welfare states may manage meaningful redistribution, perhaps equivalent to 5–15% of GDP in expanded transfers/services by 2046; the U.S. may be more politically volatile.

  16. Cost of subsistence and high-quality basics

  17. Why it matters: Median welfare depends less on nominal wages and more on whether housing, food, energy, healthcare, transport, and education become cheap.

  18. Rough guess: AI likely makes information services much cheaper; food/logistics/medicine somewhat cheaper; housing and land remain stubborn unless zoning/construction/robotics change. Housing is the biggest spoiler.

  19. Elasticity of demand for human-made or human-delivered goods

  20. Why it matters: If people strongly prefer human teachers, therapists, doctors, artists, leaders, craftspeople, and companions, human labor retains niches.

  21. Rough guess: There will be premiums, but probably not enough to employ the current professional class at current wages. Maybe 10–30% of service demand retains a meaningful human-premium component.

  22. Political responsiveness and institutional competence

  23. Why it matters: The difference between S1/S2 and S3/S4 is largely whether states can tax, regulate, maintain competition, provide services, and resist capture.
  24. Rough guess: Northern Europe, parts of East Asia, and some smaller states may adapt relatively well; the U.S. and U.K. could produce high innovation but poor distribution unless politics shifts.

5. The single most compelling causal story

The most likely determinant of the median person’s welfare in 2046 is whether AI-driven productivity growth shows up mainly as cheaper essentials and public/household income, or mainly as capital rents captured by owners of compute, land, IP, energy, and platforms. I expect AI to reduce the market value of a lot of cognitive labor before it fully automates the physical economy, so many workers could face wage pressure while still paying high prices for housing, healthcare, energy, insurance, and education. If governments respond with aggressive redistribution, public services, housing reform, competition policy, and perhaps public ownership of some AI rents, the median person is probably better off. If politics remains captured or gridlocked, GDP can rise while median security falls. So I disagree with Position A’s confidence that ordinary trade automatically preserves welfare, and I also think Position B may underweight the ability of democratic states to socialize enough gains to avoid full disempowerment.

6. Advice to this one person

  1. Shift from “writes code” to “owns outcomes.”
    Build skill in product judgment, customer discovery, system architecture, security, reliability, domain expertise, and managing AI-assisted teams. The safer engineer is not the one who types best, but the one trusted to turn ambiguous business or institutional needs into working systems.

  2. Accumulate diversified capital, not just career capital.
    If labor share falls, asset ownership matters more. Aim for a high savings rate while software wages remain good; diversify across broad equities, inflation-protected assets, and possibly housing if it fits your life and does not overleverage you. Avoid assuming your future wage growth will look like the last decade’s.

  3. Choose domains where mistakes, trust, regulation, or adversaries matter.
    Good bets include cybersecurity, AI safety/evals, infrastructure, healthcare/biotech software, finance/risk, energy, defense-adjacent resilience, compliance automation, privacy, and critical systems. These areas may use AI heavily but still need accountable humans.

  4. Maintain geographic and institutional optionality.
    Prefer locations with strong labor markets, competent public services, and plausible redistribution capacity. If you can, preserve the ability to move countries/states or work remotely. Political competence may matter as much as employer quality.

  5. Track concrete indicators instead of vibes.
    Watch: unemployment among college-educated workers, entry-level software hiring, labor share of GDP, AI company profit margins, compute concentration, housing costs relative to income, and whether governments enact serious AI taxation or public dividends. If entry-level white-collar hiring collapses for several years while AI profits soar and redistribution stalls, treat S3 as becoming much more likely.
