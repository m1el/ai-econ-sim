1. Scenario probabilities (next 20 years, to ~2046)

  • S1 BROAD PROSPERITY (20%): Productivity gains are diffused via markets or policy (e.g., UBI, expanded public goods), and human labor retains niche value (e.g., care work, creativity). Justification: Past automation (e.g., agriculture, manufacturing) initially disrupted but ultimately raised living standards, but this time may differ due to speed and generality of AI.
  • S2 MUDDLE THROUGH (35%): Partial automation creates churn; some win, others lose, but median welfare stagnates due to rent extraction and weak redistribution. Justification: Most plausible "middle ground" where institutions adapt slowly, and winners block full concentration (e.g., via regulation, unions, or demand for human services).
  • S3 CONCENTRATION (25%): Wealth/power concentrates in a small elite, median person is worse off or dependent on transfers, but humans retain some agency. Justification: Capital-biased technical change + regulatory capture + weak labor power could lock in inequality without full disempowerment.
  • S4 GRADUAL DISEMPOWERMENT (15%): Position B’s trajectory—humans lose control over institutions as AI displaces labor/cognition, even if GDP grows. Justification: Requires AI to become competitively dominant in governance, culture, and economics, which is plausible but not certain.
  • S5 OTHER (5%): AI progress stalls (3%), catastrophic misuse (1%), or transformative abundance with strong governance (1%). Justification: Stagnation is unlikely given current trends; misuse/catastrophe is a tail risk; abundance requires unprecedented coordination.

2. Where Position A is right and where it fails

Steelman:

  • Right about substitution vs. complementarity: Past automation (e.g., tractors, ATMs) destroyed jobs but created new ones. AI could do the same if it complements human labor (e.g., AI-assisted doctors, teachers) rather than replacing it entirely.
  • Right about demand: Even if labor share falls, human demand for goods/services (including human-made ones) could sustain a "parallel economy" (e.g., artisanal goods, care work, local services).
  • Right about capital accumulation: If AI is cheap and diffuse, non-elites could own/lease AI tools (e.g., small businesses using open-source models), preventing concentration.

Failure modes:

  • Capital requirements: Position A assumes the "parallel human economy" can scale, but AI may require massive capital (e.g., robotics, compute, data) that only elites can afford. Example: A farmer can buy a tractor, but can a small business afford a fleet of humanoid robots?
  • Rent extraction: If AI is not cheap/diffuse, incumbents (e.g., tech giants, landlords) capture all gains via monopolies on AI, energy, or data. Example: Uber’s driverless fleet could undercut human drivers, but humans can’t compete on cost.
  • State capacity: Position A assumes redistribution works, but if AI displaces taxable labor (e.g., payroll taxes), states may lack revenue to fund transfers. Example: If 50% of GDP is AI profits, but AI profits are hard to tax (e.g., offshore, decentralized), UBI fails.
  • Resource competition: AI could increase demand for physical resources (e.g., energy, land, rare earths), leading to scarcity for non-elites. Example: Data centers and robotics could drive up electricity prices, squeezing households.

3. Where Position B is right and where it fails

Steelman:

  • Right about feedback loops: Institutions (e.g., states, corporations) do adapt to minimize human participation when possible. Example: Corporations automate to reduce labor costs; states use AI for surveillance to reduce citizen input.
  • Right about labor share trends: Labor share of GDP has been falling for decades (from ~65% in 1970 to ~55% today in the U.S.), and AI could accelerate this. If labor share → 0%, humans lose economic leverage.
  • Right about competitive displacement: If AI is better and cheaper than humans at most tasks, humans become economically irrelevant. Example: If AI can code, write, and manage better than humans, why hire humans?
  • Right about cultural capture: Elites may prefer AI-generated culture (e.g., synthetic media, AI companions) over human-made, further marginalizing humans.

Failure modes:

  • Historical resilience of democracy: Even with inequality, democracies have sometimes corrected course (e.g., New Deal, post-WW2 welfare states). AI could trigger a similar backlash (e.g., UBI experiments, antitrust action).
  • Human demand for human goods: People may prefer human-made goods/services (e.g., art, therapy, teaching) even if AI is cheaper. Example: People pay more for "handmade" items; could apply to education, healthcare, etc.
  • Elite self-interest in stability: Even if elites could disempower humans, they may avoid it to prevent revolution or collapse. Example: Roman elites tolerated bread and circuses; modern elites may tolerate UBI.
  • AI alignment: If AI is not perfectly aligned with elite interests, it could destabilize power structures (e.g., AI-driven populism, decentralized governance).

5. The single most compelling causal story

The most likely path is S2 (Muddle Through) → S3 (Concentration), driven by capital-biased technical change + regulatory capture + weak redistribution. Here’s how:

  1. AI automates 60-80% of economically valuable tasks, but not all (e.g., care work, creativity, local services remain human).
  2. Capital share rises to 70-80% of GDP, but labor share doesn’t fall to zero because humans retain niche roles.
  3. Incumbents (tech giants, landlords, financiers) capture most gains via monopolies on AI, energy, and data.
  4. Redistribution is weak (e.g., UBI is underfunded, payroll taxes collapse), so median welfare stagnates or declines.
  5. Democracy weakens as elites use AI for surveillance and propaganda, but not enough to trigger full disempowerment (S4).
  6. The result is high GDP, high inequality, and a precarious median, with humans mostly powerless but not fully disempowered.

Why this dominates:

  • It doesn’t require AI to be perfect (just good enough to displace most labor).
  • It doesn’t require coordinated elite action (just business-as-usual rent-seeking).
  • It’s consistent with historical trends (falling labor share, rising inequality).
  • It’s robust to moderate policy responses (e.g., weak UBI, antitrust).
