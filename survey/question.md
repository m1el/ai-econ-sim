POSITION A (Perry Metzger, economist-optimist tweet thread, paraphrased): The "billionaires own all robots, the 99% starve" story misunderstands economics. If the wealthy stop needing human labor, the rest of humanity still has all its current capital, skills and productivity — they will keep trading with each other and live no worse than today. More realistically, everyone's productivity rises dramatically and everyone ends up far richer, as with every past technology.

POSITION B (Kulveit, Douglas, Ammann, Turan, Krueger, Duvenaud — "Gradual Disempowerment", arXiv 2501.16946): Societal systems (economy, states, culture) have stayed roughly aligned with human interests only because they NEEDED human labor, cognition and participation. As competitive machine alternatives displace human participation, the feedback loops that tie institutions to human flourishing break: labor share of GDP trends to zero, states funded by AI-profit taxes rather than payroll lose incentive to represent citizens, economic power buys political and cultural power, and the result is irreversible (relative or absolute) human disempowerment — without any single coordinated takeover.
