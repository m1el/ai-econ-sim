# Specialization across the scenarios: choosing skills when you can't choose the future

*Companion to [jobs in five futures](JOBS.md). That page describes the labor markets; this one is the strategy memo: which specializations perform across the whole probability distribution, written for someone who has to commit years of training before knowing which world they're in.*

**The framing that matters most:** you are not picking a job, you are picking a *portfolio position* under uncertainty. Three consequences fall out immediately:

1. **Optimize for the distribution, not the mode.** The velvet cage (C) is the single most likely future (~35%), but betting everything on it means being wrong two times in three. Good specializations are ones that are *strong in C and not ruined elsewhere*.
2. **Skills decide less than you'd like in the tails.** In B, the dividend means specialization barely matters for survival (only for status). In D and E, *no specialization saves you* — capital, jurisdiction, and relationships do. Skill choice is mostly a bet on worlds A and C — which together are ~50–55% — and on the transition decade, which happens in every scenario that isn't A.
3. **The transition decade is the real planning horizon.** Whatever 2046 looks like, 2026–2036 contains your peak earning years, the entry-level collapse, the trades boom, and the political window. Specialize for the decade you can see; convert its income into assets for the decades you can't.

## The ratings table

How ten specializations fare in each future (**++** thrives / **+** solid / **0** survives / **−** suffers / **−−** wiped out), with the panel-weighted probabilities as column headers:

| Specialization | A (~17%) | B (~25%) | C (~35%) | D (~22%) | E (~5%) | Weighted read |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **AI deployment, evals, agent-fleet ops** | − | + | ++ | + | + | The C-world winner; survives the bubble only if tied to real deployments, not hype |
| **Security (cyber + physical-systems)** | + | + | ++ | ++ | ++ | The most scenario-robust technical field on the board |
| **Safety-critical / regulated software** (medical, energy, finance, aviation) | + | + | ++ | + | 0 | Liability premium holds everywhere institutions exist |
| **Energy & grid engineering, datacenter trades** | 0 | ++ | ++ | + | ++ | Strong in every world that builds anything; the 2030s' defining boom |
| **Robotics / embodied systems** | − | ++ | ++ | + | + | High variance: dead in A, central everywhere else |
| **Licensed physical trades** (electrician, HVAC, plumber) | + | + | ++ | + | 0 | The golden-decade play; premium erodes when robotics lands late 2030s |
| **Clinical healthcare** (nurse, PA, physician) | ++ | + | ++ | + | 0 | Demographics guarantee demand in every human-centered world |
| **Care & human-premium services** (teaching, coaching, eldercare) | + | ++ | + | 0 | 0 | Unkillable demand, chronically weak wages — unless B revalues it |
| **Capital allocation / ownership** (entrepreneurship, buying equity) | 0 | + | ++ | + | 0 | Not a job — the meta-position; the only "specialization" that works in D |
| **Pure feature/SaaS development** (the 2010s default) | + | 0 | −− | −− | −− | The baseline to migrate away from; only world A is kind to it |

Three readings of the table:

- **Security is the quiet winner.** It's the only technical specialization rated + or ++ in all five worlds, because every future contains adversaries: criminals in A, fraud-at-scale in B/C, the regime and its enemies in D, sabotage and counter-sabotage in E. Conflict is scenario-invariant.
- **The energy column is almost as strong** — every world that builds anything needs watts, and even world A inherits a grid mid-upgrade.
- **The bottom row is the warning.** The most common specialization among this site's likely readers is the single worst position across the distribution.

## For the software engineer specifically

The gradient to walk, in order, each step buyable with the previous one's income:

1. **Now (2026–2029): move from writing code to running fleets.** Become the person who deploys, evaluates, secures, and takes responsibility for AI systems doing the work — evals, orchestration, incident response, integration into messy organizations. This is where software wages stay high *longest*, whatever comes after.
2. **Attach to a regulated domain.** AI-for-anything is a commodity skill; AI-for-medical-devices, AI-for-grid-control, AI-for-financial-compliance each carry the liability premium — institutions must name an accountable human, and the human who understands both the model and the regulation is scarce in every scenario with institutions.
3. **The barbell (2028–2033):** keep the high-leverage cognitive position *and* acquire one presence-based skill to journeyman level — a licensed trade, clinical certification, or hands-on hardware competence. It's a hedge that pays in C, a fallback in D, and a forcing function for understanding the physical economy in any case. The trades' golden decade gives the second arm of the barbell real standalone value.
4. **Convert relentlessly.** Every year of premium wage is a conversion window: income → broad equity, energy/infrastructure exposure, housing in a supply-constrained but governable place. The table's ninth row is the only position that performs in world D; you build it with the income from rows one and two. Your skills are the engine; the assets are the cargo; the transition decade is the voyage.
5. **Keep one foot in the human economy.** Reputation, local networks, people who'd vouch for you and trade with you — the parallel economy's currency in D/E, the meaning infrastructure in B, and cheap insurance in all worlds. It can't be automated *or* expropriated.

**What not to do:** stack a second commodity cognitive skill on the first (data analysis on top of coding is the same bet twice); chase the AI-specialist salary bubble into pure research without deployment skills (world A pops it); or let the plan depend on any single employer, sector, or country — the table's variance is the argument for never being un-diversified again.

## The honest summary

Specialization buys you the most in the worlds where institutions still function and labor still trades — A and C, roughly half the distribution. It buys survival-relevant nothing in D and E, where assets, jurisdiction, and relationships are the real positions, and it buys only status in B. So the complete strategy is layered, in declining order of controllability: **a liability-bearing, security-adjacent, AI-fluent specialization for the transition decade; aggressive conversion of its income into diversified assets; one physical skill and a strong local network as the deep hedge; and political engagement in the window, because the relative sizes of these futures are still being decided** — partly by people who understand the technology explaining it to people who write the rules.
