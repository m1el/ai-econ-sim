# openai/gpt-5.5

## 1. Trajectories

My baseline assumption: AI capabilities continue to improve quickly through the 2030s, especially in software, design, administration, tutoring, research assistance, logistics, finance, and management. Deployment is slower than raw capability improvement because of regulation, liability, legacy systems, energy/data-center constraints, organizational inertia, and the harder problem of robotics. By 2046, AI is economically central in developed economies, but not an effortless post-scarcity technology. Physical-world automation is substantial but uneven.

### 1. Managed abundance with tense but successful redistribution — 32%

**Causal mechanism:**  
AI raises productivity strongly in tradable services, health administration, education, engineering, logistics, drug discovery, software, and parts of manufacturing. Governments respond with heavier taxation of profits, land, carbon, compute, or capital gains; expanded transfers; public AI infrastructure; and wage subsidies. Competition policy prevents total platform lock-in. Housing and energy constraints are partly relaxed through permitting reform, modular construction, grid expansion, and cheaper storage.

**Median person’s economic life in 2046:**  
The median working-age person still works, but fewer hours: perhaps 25–32 hours/week on average. Work is more supervisory, interpersonal, maintenance-oriented, care-oriented, or local-service-oriented. Many people use AI agents as normal tools. Median disposable income is materially higher than in 2026, perhaps 20–45% higher in real terms, though headline wages may not rise as much as total disposable income. Income comes from wages, refundable tax credits, child or housing benefits, public health subsidies, and possibly small citizen-dividend-like payments. Healthcare and education are cheaper or more effective. Housing remains expensive in superstar metros but less crushing than in the 2010s–2020s. Economic security is better for the median person than today, though status competition and anxiety about replaceability remain high.

### 2. High-productivity rentier economy — 26%

**Causal mechanism:**  
AI generates large productivity gains, but the gains accrue mainly to owners of compute, models, data, robotics supply chains, intellectual property, land, and dominant platforms. Labor’s bargaining power weakens. Regulation favors incumbents because safety, security, and compliance requirements are expensive. Governments do redistribute somewhat, but not enough to offset concentration. Asset prices rise faster than wages.

**Median person’s economic life in 2046:**  
The median person has access to astonishingly capable AI services, cheap digital entertainment, better diagnostics, and more personalized education, but economic autonomy is weaker. Full-time employment is less secure; many people cycle through contract work, public-service jobs, platform-mediated local work, eldercare, compliance, security, or “human trust” roles. Disposable income may be modestly higher than in 2026, but rent, insurance, housing, and subscription-like access to key services consume much of the gain. A large fraction of income comes from public transfers or quasi-transfers, but wages still matter for most households. The median person feels technologically rich but institutionally dependent.

### 3. Bottlenecked productivity and expensive essentials — 19%

**Causal mechanism:**  
AI improves many tasks but runs into bottlenecks: slow organizational adoption, energy constraints, weak grid buildout, data-center limits, regulatory fragmentation, liability risks, cyberattacks, and difficulty automating physical-world work. Productivity growth improves only modestly versus 2006–2026. Meanwhile, housing scarcity, aging populations, healthcare demand, climate adaptation costs, and public debt keep essential costs high.

**Median person’s economic life in 2046:**  
The median person uses AI constantly, but it feels like “better software” rather than an economic revolution. Work remains necessary and often stressful. Many jobs are reorganized rather than eliminated. Real disposable income is only slightly higher or roughly flat versus 2026. Housing, care, insurance, and taxes are major burdens. AI tutors, assistants, and health tools improve quality of life, but the median household still worries about rent/mortgage payments, retirement, eldercare, and job continuity. Security is not dramatically worse than in 2026, but the sense of failed promise is widespread.

### 4. Labor-market rupture and political compensation after delay — 14%

**Causal mechanism:**  
AI substitutes for a wide range of cognitive labor faster than institutions adapt. White-collar employment in administration, software, media, finance, legal support, accounting, customer support, and some management functions contracts sharply. Robotics also begins to displace warehouse, transport, food-service, and basic maintenance work. Initially, governments underreact; then political pressure forces large transfer programs, public employment, work-sharing, or AI-dividend schemes.

**Median person’s economic life in 2046:**  
The median working-age household receives a large share of income from government transfers, wage supplements, or public job guarantees. Paid employment exists but is rationed, unstable, or socially segmented. Some people with scarce human skills, credentials, ownership stakes, or public-sector positions do well; many others depend on transfers. Disposable income may be protected from collapse, but dignity, bargaining power, and career progression are weaker. Domestic AI and robotics are common, but ownership and maintenance costs still matter. Politics is dominated by distributional conflict over who owns the automated economy.

### 5. Fragmented defensive economies — 9%

**Causal mechanism:**  
AI development coincides with serious geopolitical, cyber, climate, migration, and fiscal shocks. Governments prioritize security, industrial policy, surveillance, supply-chain control, and resilience over efficiency. Cross-border trade in compute, chips, models, talent, energy, and data fragments. AI is powerful but often militarized, regulated, or constrained by national-security concerns. Productivity gains are partly consumed by defense, adaptation, and institutional breakdown.

**Median person’s economic life in 2046:**  
The median person lives in a more protected but less open economy. Some AI services are excellent, but access is shaped by national systems and security rules. Taxes are higher, travel and trade are more restricted, and governments play a larger direct role in allocating energy, housing, healthcare, and employment. Real disposable income is flat or lower than in 2026 in several developed countries. Essentials take a larger budget share. Economic security depends heavily on citizenship, region, and public-sector attachment. Political voice may be weaker because emergencies justify centralized decision-making.

## 2. Calibrated estimates

Point probabilities for developed economies in 2046, treating each statement independently:

- **a) Median real disposable income, including transfers and adjusted for cost of living, is higher than in 2026:** **72%**
- **b) Median real disposable income is more than 20% lower than in 2026:** **8%**
- **c) The share of national income flowing to labor is below 40%, versus roughly 55–60% today:** **18%**
- **d) More than 25% of prime-age adults who want paid work cannot find any:** **10%**
- **e) Ordinary citizens’ influence over government policy is substantially weaker than in 2026:** **55%**
- **f) The top 1%’s share of national wealth is at least 10 percentage points higher than in 2026:** **32%**
- **g) Housing, energy, and food together consume a larger share of the median household budget than in 2026:** **43%**
- **h) A typical household can buy a robot/AI system that performs most domestic and basic production tasks for less than 6 months of median household income:** **45%**
- **i) Government transfers, or equivalents like citizen dividends, are the largest income source for the median working-age household:** **22%**
- **j) AI systems or the organizations operating them make most consequential resource-allocation decisions in the economy with little effective human oversight:** **28%**

## 3. Drivers

1. **Annual effective AI capability improvement in economically relevant tasks**
   - **Why it matters:** The key question is not benchmark performance but how quickly AI becomes cheaper and better than human labor in real workflows: coding, analysis, design, customer service, operations, compliance, logistics, management, and research.
   - **Best guess:** Rapid through the early-to-mid 2030s, then slower but still meaningful. By 2046, AI performs a majority of cognitive task-hours that were paid human work in 2026, but not all tasks and not always autonomously.

2. **Cost of reliable AI labor relative to human labor**
   - **Why it matters:** Displacement and productivity depend on whether AI is merely useful or actually cheaper, dependable, insurable, and scalable.
   - **Best guess:** For many digital tasks, the cost of AI labor falls to less than 10% of comparable developed-economy human labor by the late 2030s. For embodied physical tasks, costs fall more slowly and remain heterogeneous.

3. **Robotics deployment cost and reliability**
   - **Why it matters:** If robots become cheap and general-purpose, AI affects not only office work but also construction, eldercare, food preparation, cleaning, farming, warehousing, and local services. That is the difference between “software productivity boom” and deeper labor-market transformation.
   - **Best guess:** By 2046, robots are common in warehouses, factories, hospitals, farms, eldercare facilities, and affluent homes. A mass-market domestic robot is plausible, but “does most household and basic production tasks” remains uncertain.

4. **Energy and compute expansion**
   - **Why it matters:** AI deployment at economy-wide scale requires electricity, chips, data centers, cooling, grid connections, and capital investment. Energy abundance also determines whether productivity gains translate into cheaper goods or are eaten by infrastructure costs.
   - **Best guess:** Developed economies expand electricity generation substantially, led by renewables, storage, gas backup, nuclear life extensions, and some new nuclear/geothermal. Grid buildout remains a bottleneck. AI compute grows dramatically but is periodically constrained.

5. **Market concentration in AI, cloud, chips, data, and robotics**
   - **Why it matters:** If AI is competitive and broadly accessible, gains pass through as lower prices and higher wages. If it is concentrated, gains become rents to a small set of firms, investors, and states.
   - **Best guess:** High concentration persists in frontier models, chips, cloud infrastructure, and robotics platforms, but open-source and state-backed alternatives prevent a single global monopoly. Rent concentration is serious but not total.

6. **Fiscal capacity and willingness to redistribute**
   - **Why it matters:** If labor income weakens, median welfare depends heavily on whether governments can tax AI-driven rents, capital gains, land, consumption, or compute and recycle revenue to households.
   - **Best guess:** Redistribution expands, but unevenly. Northern and Western Europe, Canada, Australia, and parts of East Asia likely do more. The United States likely expands transfers too, but in a more fragmented, politically contested way.

7. **Housing supply elasticity in high-productivity regions**
   - **Why it matters:** Even large productivity gains can be absorbed by land rents if people must live near scarce jobs, schools, care networks, and safe neighborhoods. Housing is the main channel through which abundance can fail to reach the median household.
   - **Best guess:** Some permitting reform and construction automation improve supply, but not enough in the most desirable metros. Housing costs remain a major drag on median living standards.

8. **Political control over automated decision systems**
   - **Why it matters:** If allocation of jobs, credit, insurance, healthcare, education, policing, benefits, and media attention is delegated to opaque AI systems controlled by states or firms, formal income may rise while actual autonomy falls.
   - **Best guess:** AI-mediated allocation becomes pervasive. Oversight improves compared with today’s algorithmic systems, but remains weaker than advertised. Citizens gain convenience and lose some practical agency.

## 4. The mechanism you'd bet on

The mechanism I would bet on is the pass-through rate from AI-driven productivity to median purchasing power. AI will probably make developed economies capable of producing much more output per hour of human labor, especially in digital and administrative domains. But the median person benefits only if those gains pass through via lower prices, higher wages, broader ownership, public services, or transfers rather than being captured by owners of scarce complements: compute, models, data, chips, land, energy infrastructure, regulatory licenses, and political influence. The most predictive variable is therefore not raw AI capability; it is whether competition and public policy convert automated productivity into cheap essentials and reliable household income.

## 5. Confidence and failure

Evidence that would make my central forecast wrong would include: clear signs by 2028–2030 that frontier AI capability growth has plateaued despite rising compute; failure of AI systems to become reliable in messy enterprise workflows; robotics remaining far too expensive or fragile for broad deployment by the mid-2030s; energy and chip constraints binding much harder than expected; or, in the opposite direction, a sudden arrival of cheap general-purpose robots and highly autonomous AI firms before 2035. I would also update strongly if major developed governments either successfully implemented broad AI dividends early, making inequality less severe than expected, or instead lost the practical ability to tax and regulate AI rents.

The claims I am least confident in are **h**, because domestic robotics has repeatedly disappointed and may either lag badly or arrive suddenly; **j**, because “little effective human oversight” is institutionally hard to define and could vary sharply by country; and **c**, because measured labor share may become less informative if households receive income through mixed channels such as platform dividends, sovereign wealth funds, wage subsidies, or AI-tax rebates. My highest-confidence claim is that AI will be economically central by 2046, but I am less confident about whether that centrality produces broad-based security or concentrated dependence.
