# Economic Forecast for Developed Economies, 2026–2046

**My AI assumptions (stated upfront):** I assume AI capability continues to advance steadily-to-rapidly, with broad deployment lagging capability by 5-15 years due to organizational inertia, regulation, physical-world bottlenecks (robotics, energy, supply chains), and trust. My modal view: by 2046, AI automates most cognitive routine work and a large fraction of physical work, but full economy-wide autonomy is incomplete. I treat a hard "intelligence explosion" before 2046 as possible but not modal (~15-20%). These assumptions drive everything below.

## 1. Trajectories

**A. Broad-based augmentation (28%)**
*Mechanism:* AI raises productivity, but complementary human labor remains valuable, new task categories emerge faster than old ones vanish, and bargaining institutions plus political pressure distribute some gains. Deployment bottlenecks slow displacement enough for adjustment.
*Median life in 2046:* Works ~30 hrs/week in a hybrid human-AI role (care, skilled trades, supervision, interpersonal services). Real income ~25-40% above 2026. Healthcare and education cheaper per unit; housing still expensive in dense metros. Economic security moderate—employer-based but supplemented by expanded transfers.

**B. Polarized abundance (26%)**
*Mechanism:* Aggregate output booms but capital and a shrinking skilled cohort capture most gains. Labor share falls; cost of goods/services drops sharply while positional goods (land, status, elite access) inflate. Transfers cushion but don't equalize.
*Median life in 2046:* Patchwork income—part-time/gig work plus meaningful government transfer. Material goods abundant and cheap; housing, healthcare access, and "human-delivered" premium services strain budgets. Feels materially comfortable but economically powerless and anxious.

**C. Managed redistribution (18%)**
*Mechanism:* Displacement is rapid enough to force political response—citizen dividends, sovereign-wealth-style ownership stakes, expanded public provision. Wealth concentration triggers redistributive backlash that partially succeeds.
*Median life in 2046:* Transfers/dividends are a major income source; paid work optional or part-time and often chosen for meaning. Real consumption higher than 2026. Security high if institutions hold; dependent on fiscal/political stability.

**D. Stagnant drift (15%)**
*Mechanism:* AI underdelivers economically—deployment stalls on regulation, trust, energy, integration costs; productivity gains stay modest (à la prior tech disappointments). Aging demographics and debt drag on growth.
*Median life in 2046:* Looks much like 2026, slightly richer or flatter. Continued housing/health cost pressure. Familiar anxieties, no transformation.

**E. Concentrated capture / democratic erosion (13%)**
*Mechanism:* AI-enabled productivity accrues to a narrow elite who also gain disproportionate political and coercive power, weakening the median person's leverage (their labor and votes matter less). Redistribution fails.
*Median life in 2046:* Real incomes flat-to-lower, precarious work or dependence on discretionary (not entitled) transfers. Reduced political voice. Material goods cheap but security and agency low.

## 3. Drivers

1. **Pace of robotics/physical-world automation** — Cognitive automation alone leaves much labor demand; physical automation determines whether displacement is broad. *Best guess:* steady progress, meaningful but incomplete by 2046.
2. **Elasticity of new task creation** — Whether AI generates new human-complementary roles faster than it eliminates old ones. *Best guess:* positive but slowing relative to history.
3. **Labor share trajectory** — Directly sets median wage outcomes. *Best guess:* declines to ~45-50%, not collapse.
4. **Fiscal/political capacity to redistribute** — Determines whether displaced gains reach the median. *Best guess:* partial, reactive, uneven across countries.
5. **Housing supply elasticity** — The dominant non-tradable cost; AI doesn't easily make land. *Best guess:* remains constrained; positional inflation persists.
6. **Energy cost and abundance** — Gates both AI scaling and household cost relief. *Best guess:* gradually cheaper (solar/storage), not transformatively by 2046.
7. **Wealth/ownership concentration rate** — Who owns the AI capital stock. *Best guess:* concentration rises absent intervention.
8. **Degree of human oversight retained** — Political/regulatory insistence on human-in-the-loop. *Best guess:* substantial oversight retained in consequential domains through 2046.

## 4. The mechanism you'd bet on

**Whether productivity gains reach the median person depends primarily on the distribution of asset ownership and the political capacity to redistribute, not on aggregate output.** AI will almost certainly raise total production; the median person's welfare hinges on whether they hold a claim on that production—via still-valuable labor, broadened capital ownership, or robust transfers. If labor's bargaining power erodes faster than ownership broadens or redistribution expands, abundance and immiseration coexist. The decisive variable is institutional/political, not technological: societies that convert AI productivity into citizen claims (dividends, public equity, strong transfers) will see rising median welfare; those that don't will see polarization regardless of how rich they become in aggregate.

## 5. Confidence and failure

**What would make my central forecast wrong:**
- A faster-than-expected intelligence/robotics explosion (2030s) would collapse my deployment-lag assumption, pushing probability mass toward trajectories B and E and raising (c), (d), (j) sharply.
- Evidence that AI *fails* to deliver productivity gains by ~2032 (flat TFP, deployment stalling) would shift mass toward D, lowering (a)'s upside and most transformation-dependent items.
- Faster political mobilization for redistribution than I expect would raise (i) and trajectory C.

**Claims I'm least confident in:**
- **(c) Labor share below 40% (40%)** — genuinely a coin flip; I have wide uncertainty on the speed of labor displacement.
- **(h) Affordable general robot (35%)** — heavily dependent on robotics hardware cost curves I can't forecast well; could be very wrong either direction.
- **(e) Weakened citizen influence (52%)** — barely better than a guess; conflates economic and political dynamics that may decouple.
- **(i) Transfers as largest income source (30%)** — depends entirely on the politics of redistribution, which is my single largest source of model uncertainty.

My genuine epistemic state: I'm fairly confident output rises and reasonably confident the median person's *material consumption* rises (a, 68%); I'm most uncertain about *distribution, security, and agency*, where technology underdetermines outcomes and politics dominates.
