# anthropic/claude-fable-5 (fresh instance, no conversation context)

## Assumptions (stated up front)

My central assumption: AI capability progress continues at roughly the 2022–2025 pace through ~2030 (driven by compute scaling, algorithmic gains, and inference-time techniques), then decelerates somewhat as compute scaling hits capital/energy constraints. By the early-to-mid 2030s, AI systems can perform most well-specified cognitive work at expert level. **Deployment lags capability by 5–15 years** because of organizational inertia, regulation, liability, and integration costs — this lag, not capability, is the main uncertainty. Capable general-purpose robotics lags cognitive AI by roughly 5–10 years and is cost-constrained longer. I put ~10% weight on a hard plateau (no economically transformative AI by 2046) and ~10% on a discontinuity (recursive self-improvement producing >10%/yr growth or a regime change in who controls the economy).

## 1. Trajectories

**T1: Productivity boom, messy but managed transition — 30%.**
*Mechanism:* AI diffuses into knowledge work through the 2030s, adding 1–2pp to annual TFP growth. Large occupational churn (10–25% of jobs substantially restructured per decade — fast by historical standards but comparable to past upheavals stretched over less time). Tight fiscal pressure from demographics forces governments to tax the AI surplus; redistribution expands incrementally (wage insurance, expanded EITC-style programs, sectoral retraining), not via UBI.
*Median person in 2046:* Works ~30–35 hrs/week, mostly in care, trades, hospitality, supervision/liability roles, or AI-complemented services. Real income ~40–70% above 2026. Many goods and services (software, media, legal/medical advice, education) near-free; housing and human-touch services remain expensive. Economic security moderately better than today; transfers are a larger but not dominant income share.

**T2: Steady diffusion — AI as electricity, not as a rupture — 28%.**
*Mechanism:* Capabilities are real but the deployment lag dominates: complementary investment, regulation, and the difficulty of automating tacit/physical/relational work mean TFP gains of only ~0.5–1pp above baseline. Labor markets adjust the way they did to IT — gradual task recomposition, no mass displacement.
*Median person in 2046:* Life recognizably like 2026 plus 20–35% real income growth. Standard employment remains the core income source. AI is embedded everywhere (every worker has agentic assistants) but the wage structure, benefit systems, and housing problems look familiar. Demographics make pensions and healthcare the dominant political fights, not AI.

**T3: Capital-concentrated decoupling — 20%.**
*Mechanism:* Strong AI plus weak institutional response. Compute, data, and frontier models are owned by a small number of firms; labor's bargaining power erodes faster than redistribution scales up. GDP grows briskly while median wages stagnate or fall; the labor share drops toward 40–45%. Political systems are captured or gridlocked enough that transfers grow only defensively.
*Median person in 2046:* Patchwork income — part-time service work, gig/platform tasks, modest transfers. Real income roughly flat vs. 2026 despite a much richer economy. Cheap AI-produced goods mask stagnation somewhat, but housing and status goods are further out of reach. Pervasive insecurity; politics is angry and volatile. This trajectory is unstable — it likely resolves into T1 (via political correction) or something worse within a decade or two of onset.

**T4: Plateau and demographic drag — 12%.**
*Mechanism:* AI progress stalls on reliability, agency, or economic value short of broad automation; it becomes a useful productivity tool worth ~0.2–0.5pp of growth. Aging, debt overhang, and slow productivity dominate. Developed economies grow ~1%/yr per capita.
*Median person in 2046:* Works longer (retirement age 69+), pays higher taxes for elder care, real income up maybe 10–20%. Japan-style stability: low unemployment, low dynamism, persistent fiscal strain.

**T5: Discontinuity (explosive growth or control regime change) — 10%.**
*Mechanism:* AI R&D automation triggers a feedback loop; growth exceeds 10%/yr, or alternatively effective control of the economy passes to AI systems/the entities running them faster than governance can adapt. Outcomes are bimodal: near-abundance with dividends, or severe disempowerment of ordinary people.
*Median person in 2046:* Either material abundance with income dominated by state/sovereign-fund dividends and work optional, or formal comfort with near-zero economic and political leverage. I can't assign confident internal weights; call it 60/40 toward the better branch conditional on T5.

## 2. Calibrated estimates

- e) Citizens' policy influence substantially weaker than 2026: **35%**
- f) Top 1% wealth share at least 10pp higher: **15%**
- g) Housing+energy+food larger share of median budget: **30%**
- h) Capable domestic robot for <6 months median income: **35%**
- i) Transfers the largest income source for median working-age household: **12%**
- j) AI/operators make most consequential allocation decisions with little effective human oversight: **30%** (note: a weaker version — AI deeply embedded in most allocation decisions with nominal human sign-off — I'd put at ~70%)

## 3. Drivers

1. **TFP growth contribution from AI (pp/yr).** The single biggest determinant of the size of the pie. Best guess: ~0.3pp by 2030, ~1pp average 2030–2046, with fat tails both ways.
2. **Deployment lag (years from capability to majority adoption in affected sectors).** Determines whether change is absorbable churn or a shock. Best guess: 8–12 years, slowly compressing.
3. **Task substitution vs. complementarity elasticity for median-skill labor.** Decides whether AI raises or destroys median wages. Best guess: net complementary through the early 2030s, increasingly substitutive after; physical/relational tasks remain a wage floor through 2046.
4. **Robotics cost curve ($/hour of general manipulation).** Gates whether automation reaches the ~40% of work that is physical. Best guess: falls ~15–25%/yr; crosses median-wage parity for many tasks in the late 2030s.
5. **Effective redistribution rate (share of AI-attributable surplus reaching the bottom 80%).** Separates T1 from T3. Best guess: starts low (<20%), rises under political pressure; the lag here is the main welfare risk.
6. **Energy price trajectory ($/MWh real).** Constrains both compute scaling and the cost of abundance. Best guess: flat-to-declining after 2032 as solar+storage and possibly advanced nuclear scale; transient spikes from AI demand in the late 2020s.
7. **Housing supply elasticity.** Housing is the main channel by which productivity gains fail to reach median living standards. Best guess: modest improvement (YIMBY reforms + AI-cheapened construction), insufficient in superstar cities.
8. **Concentration of frontier AI (number of independent actors at the frontier).** Determines rent capture and political-economy risk. Best guess: 3–6 firms plus 2–3 states; open-weight models 1–3 years behind, which meaningfully limits rents.

## 4. The mechanism you'd bet on

The mechanism I'd bet on is the **race between the diffusion speed of labor-substituting AI and the adaptation speed of redistributive and labor-market institutions**. The median person's 2046 welfare is not primarily determined by how capable AI becomes — in almost all my trajectories the economy is much richer — but by whether the surplus reaches them through wages in residual labor-demanding sectors, falling prices, or transfers, *before* their bargaining power erodes. Historically, institutions adapt over 15–40 years (factory acts, the New Deal, postwar welfare states), while AI diffusion looks set to run on a 10–15 year clock. If the gap is small, you get T1/T2; if it's large, you get a decade or more of T3 even in a booming economy. That single ratio — diffusion speed over institutional response speed — predicts the median outcome better than any capability forecast.

## 5. Confidence and failure

**What would prove me wrong:**
- *Toward plateau (T4):* If by 2029–2031 AI agents still cannot reliably complete multi-day, loosely specified professional tasks, and measured productivity in AI-adopting sectors shows no acceleration, I'd shift 20–25 points from T1/T3/T5 toward T2/T4.
- *Toward rupture (T3/T5):* If by 2030 frontier labs demonstrate largely automated AI R&D, or enterprise adoption compresses to a 2–4 year lag with visible white-collar employment declines in national statistics, I'd roughly double T3+T5.
- *On distribution:* Early evidence on whether AI compresses or widens wage distributions (current evidence weakly suggests compression — it helps lower performers most) is highly diagnostic for T1 vs. T3.

**Least confident claims:** (1) My deployment-lag estimate (8–12 years) — it's anchored on IT/electricity history, and AI may be different because it can drive its own integration; this assumption load-bears for most of my trajectory weights. (2) Estimate (h), the household robot — I have wide uncertainty on robotics cost curves and could see anywhere from 15% to 60%. (3) Estimate (e), citizen influence — it depends on hard-to-model political dynamics, and I may be underweighting both AI-enabled surveillance/persuasion (worse) and AI-enabled civic tools (better). (4) The implicit assumption that developed-economy political systems correct T3-style decoupling within a decade or two; the historical base rate supports it, but historical precedents involve labor that retained economic leverage to withhold — which is exactly what AI erodes. That last point is the soft spot in my entire central forecast.
