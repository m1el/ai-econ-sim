# moonshotai/kimi-k2.6

 **Assumed AI path:** Cognitive automation advances steadily through 2035—multimodal foundation models automate routine analysis, communication, and coding—but general-purpose robotics and autonomous scientific discovery remain harder than pure information processing. No hard takeoff or singleton AGI. Deployment lags capability by 5–8 years due to energy, liability, and organizational friction. After 2035, progress continues but is increasingly constrained by physical-world bottlenecks and regulatory response. AI is a strong substitute for median cognitive labor but a weaker substitute for physical dexterity, social/emotional labor, and local tacit knowledge.

## 1. Trajectories

**Trajectory 1: Institutional Lag (40%)**
Causal mechanism: AI raises aggregate productivity by 40–60% over 20 years, but legal, land-use, and tax institutions adapt slowly. Capital captures most productivity gains in tradable sectors, while non-tradable sectors—housing, healthcare, education, and energy infrastructure—absorb them via Baumol cost disease and regulatory rents. Governments expand transfers and tax credits but cannot overcome NIMBYism, international tax competition, or the political power of incumbent asset owners.

Median person in 2046: Works 30–32 hours per week in a hybrid human-AI role—AI handles documentation, scheduling, analysis, and routine communication; the human provides physical presence, emotional labor, or local coordination (care work, trades, logistics oversight, community services). Income sources: 55% wages from formal hybrid jobs, 30% from government transfers and refundable tax credits, 15% from informal or platform gig work. Real disposable income is 5–10% above 2026 levels, but housing and healthcare now consume 50–55% of the household budget (up from ~45%). Subjective economic security is lower than in 2026 despite better digital tools, because career trajectories have dissolved and housing wealth is permanently out of reach.

**Trajectory 2: Abundance with Friction (25%)**
Causal mechanism: AI drives breakthroughs in materials science and energy (advanced geothermal, cheap solar/storage, or early fusion), collapsing the cost of food, energy, and manufactured goods. Open-source models and competitive global markets prevent total rent capture in tradables. However, housing remains expensive due to zoning and location rents, and professional licensing barriers persist in healthcare and education.

Median person in 2046: Real disposable income is 25–35% above 2026 levels, but consumption baskets are bifurcated: energy, food, electronics, and transport are trivially cheap, while a decent apartment in a desirable location still costs 35–40% of income. Works 20–25 hours per week in creative, community, or care roles where human presence carries status value. Home includes narrow AI appliances and cheap domestic robots for cleaning, cooking, and basic maintenance, but general-purpose humanoid help remains a luxury. Political conflict shifts from wages to land ownership and access to unspoiled physical space.

**Trajectory 3: Rentier Dystopia (20%)**
Causal mechanism: AI deployment is rapid and protected by tight IP, data moats, and platform lock-in. Regulatory capture blocks redistribution; international coordination on taxation fails. Labor share collapses as AI disintermediates professions and routine management. Wealth concentrates in owners of compute infrastructure, energy, and algorithmic IP. Formal democracies survive as procedural shells managed by AI-assisted surveillance and micro-targeted propaganda.

Median person in 2046: The median working-age household receives 60% of income from means-tested digital transfers and in-kind credits, 30% from sporadic gig work in “human premium” services (therapeutic care, artisanal production, live entertainment), and 10% from informal barter. 20–30% of prime-age adults who want formal work cannot find it. Lives in a small, algorithmically allocated rental unit with AI-managed entertainment, telemedicine, and basic material needs met, but holds no assets, enjoys no career trajectory, and faces heavy behavioral surveillance. Subjective security is minimal; social mobility has evaporated.

**Trajectory 4: The Long Plateau (15%)**
Causal mechanism: AI capabilities plateau around 2030–2035—scaling laws hit data-quality and energy limits, robotics fails to generalize across physical tasks, and the productivity surge disappoints. Developed economies face demographic collapse, sovereign debt overhang, and climate adaptation costs without a compensating technology boom. AI becomes a better search engine and chatbot but does not transform physical production.

Median person in 2046: Real disposable income is flat or 5–10% below 2026 levels. AI is present but economically peripheral. The median person works 40+ hours per week in deteriorating public services, logistics, and manual maintenance. Retirement ages have risen; pension systems are strained. Climate impacts have raised insurance, food, and cooling costs. Political fragmentation and inter-generational conflict are high. The dominant mood is not techno-optimism or techno-dread, but managed decline.

## 3. Drivers

1. **Elasticity of substitution between AI/capital and cognitive labor.** Why it matters: Determines whether labor share collapses or stabilizes. If AI is a near-perfect substitute for routine cognitive work, median wages in tradable sectors fall toward the reservation wage unless redistribution is massive. Best-guess trajectory: 1.2–1.5 in digital/tradable sectors (strong substitution), 0.6–0.8 in non-tradable services (complementarity in care, trades, and local coordination). Net effect: partial labor share decline but not total collapse.

2. **Real cost of dispatchable clean energy ($/MWh for firm 24/7 power).** Why it matters: AI training, robotics, desalination, and vertical farming are energy-hungry. Cheap energy is the critical input that converts digital intelligence into physical abundance. Best-guess trajectory: $40–50/MWh by 2046, a 40–50% decline from 2026 firm-power costs. Meaningful but not revolutionary.
