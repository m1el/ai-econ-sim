Core AI Assumption: I assume AI development will be accelerating in software/cognition but physically constrained in hardware/deployment. We will see rapid advances in autonomous cognitive agents and digital automation by the early 2030s, but physical robotics, energy grid build-outs, and supply chain reconfigurations will lag significantly due to real-world friction, thermodynamic limits, and regulatory bottlenecks.

1. Trajectories

1. The Automated Welfare State (Probability: 40%) * Causal Mechanism: AI drives massive productivity gains in digital, administrative, and professional services, displacing a large fraction of white-collar work. However, physical automation lags, keeping the cost of physical goods, housing, and care work high. To prevent social unrest, mass default, and a collapse in aggregate demand, governments are forced to implement large-scale wealth redistribution mechanisms (e.g., UBI, negative income taxes, or sovereign wealth dividends) funded by taxing AI compute, corporate revenues, and land value. * Median Life in 2046: The median person works 15–20 hours a week in "human-in-the-loop" oversight, physical trades, or empathetic care work. 60% of their income comes from government transfers. Digital goods, entertainment, and basic manufactured items are virtually free, but housing and energy consume the majority of their earned wages. They enjoy high baseline material security but very low upward economic mobility.

2. Neo-Feudal Technocracy (Probability: 30%) * Causal Mechanism: AI and robotics advance rapidly and are tightly captured by a small oligopoly of tech and industrial conglomerates. Regulatory capture and capital mobility prevent governments from implementing meaningful wealth redistribution. Labor's share of income collapses as cognitive and physical automation scale. The state remains structurally too weak or too captured to enforce broad welfare, leading to a bifurcated society. * Median Life in 2046: The median person lives a precarious "tasker" existence. Income is highly volatile, derived from micro-tasks AI cannot yet do, physical gig-work, or renting out personal data/attention. 80% of their income goes to rent, corporate subscriptions, and basic sustenance provided by the monopolies. Security is tied to corporate patronage (e.g., living in company-subsidized housing in exchange for data and loyalty), resembling a high-tech feudalism.

3. Augmented Stagnation / The Muddle-Through (Probability: 20%) * Causal Mechanism: AI hits a data, compute, or algorithmic wall; scaling laws break down sooner than expected. We achieve highly effective copilots, but fully autonomous, reliable agents remain elusive. Productivity growth remains at historical trends (1-2% annually). Meanwhile, severe demographic aging eats up the productivity gains in healthcare and eldercare costs, resulting in a feeling of economic stagnation despite better technology. * Median Life in 2046: The median person works 35–40 hours a week in a heavily AI-augmented office or physical job. Real income is slightly higher than in 2026, but the gains are entirely consumed by skyrocketing healthcare, eldercare, and housing costs. Security is tied to traditional employment benefits. Daily life feels much like 2024, just with better screens, more automated customer service, and a visibly older population.

4. Post-Scarcity Transition (Probability: 10%) * Causal Mechanism: AGI is achieved by the early 2030s and successfully solves the physical constraints of robotics, materials science, and clean energy (e.g., commercializing advanced geothermal or fusion). The cost of both cognitive and physical labor approaches zero. The economy transitions from a scarcity-based allocation model to an abundance-based model. * Median Life in 2046: Paid work is entirely voluntary. The concept of "income" is largely moot; access to resources is allocated via sheer abundance or a robust, universally accepted citizen dividend. Housing is 3D-printed in days, and energy is virtually free. The median person enjoys extremely high material security but faces profound philosophical and psychological adjustments regarding human purpose and status.

3. Drivers

  1. AI Compute Elasticity (Scaling Law Exponent): The rate at which additional compute translates to model capability. Why it matters: It determines whether we achieve AGI or plateau at "very good copilots." Best-guess trajectory: Diminishing returns post-2028; the exponent drops from ~1.0 to ~0.5, meaning we need exponentially more compute for marginal cognitive gains.
  2. Humanoid Robot Unit Cost at Scale: The manufacturing cost of a general-purpose bipedal robot capable of unstructured physical labor. Why it matters: It dictates the speed of physical automation and the cost of physical services. Best-guess trajectory: Drops to $30,000–$40,000 by 2040 (roughly the cost of a cheap car today), making them viable for commercial use but too expensive for ubiquitous household adoption.
  3. Firm Energy Cost per kWh: The cost of 24/7 reliable, dispatchable electricity. Why it matters: AI data centers and robotic fleets are intensely energy-hungry; physical constraints on the grid will bottleneck AI deployment. Best-guess trajectory: Rises slightly in real terms to $0.12–$0.15/kWh in developed nations due to grid congestion and transmission bottlenecks, despite cheap generation from solar/wind.
  4. Labor Share of National Income: The percentage of GDP paid to workers as wages and benefits. Why it matters: It is the direct mathematical expression of the balance of power between capital (AI/robotics owners) and labor. Best-guess trajectory: Declines steadily from ~55% today to 45% by 2046 as cognitive automation suppresses wage growth.
  5. Effective Tax Rate on AI Compute and Capital Gains: The actual percentage of AI-driven corporate profits and capital appreciation collected by the state. Why it matters: It determines the state's capacity to fund the transfers required to prevent the Neo-Feudal trajectory. Best-guess trajectory: Rises to 25–30% (from lower current effective rates) due to intense political pressure, but avoids confiscatory levels that would trigger capital flight.
  6. Old-Age Dependency Ratio: The number of retirees (65+) per 100 working-age adults (15-64). Why it matters: It drives the fiscal burden of pensions and the structural demand for healthcare/eldercare, which are highly resistant to automation. Best-guess trajectory: Reaches 45–50 in most developed nations (up from ~30 today), acting as a massive deflationary drag on disposable income.

4. The mechanism you'd bet on

The single most predictive causal mechanism for the median person's economic welfare in 2046 is the divergence between digital deflation and physical inflation (Baumol’s cost disease on steroids). The median person's welfare will be determined not by how cheap digital intelligence, software, and entertainment become, but by the political and technological mechanisms used to manage the skyrocketing relative cost of physical and spatial goods—specifically housing, land, energy, and human-centric care. If physical bottlenecks are solved via radical housing deregulation, grid expansion, and cheap robotics, or if they are heavily subsidized by the state, welfare soars; if physical goods are left to market pricing while cognitive wages stagnate, welfare collapses into a high-tech subsistence trap where the median person can afford infinite virtual reality but cannot afford a physical home or a human nurse.

5. Confidence and failure

My central forecast would be proven wrong if AI scaling laws completely break down by 2027 (e.g., no further capability gains despite a 100x increase in compute), which would invalidate my "accelerating software" assumption and push us firmly into the Augmented Stagnation trajectory. Conversely, if a fully autonomous humanoid robot is demonstrated doing unstructured, multi-hour physical labor reliably for under $10,000 by 2029, my "physically constrained" assumption fails, rapidly accelerating us toward the Post-Scarcity Transition. I am least confident in my estimates for statement (i) (government transfers becoming the largest income source) and statement (h) (cheap household robots). The political economy of implementing massive UBI/transfer programs is incredibly messy, and I may be overestimating state capacity and underestimating institutional inertia. Similarly, hardware and robotics timelines are notoriously unpredictable; the physical world is vastly more complex than the digital world, and I might be overly optimistic about the cost-reduction curve for general-purpose physical robots.
