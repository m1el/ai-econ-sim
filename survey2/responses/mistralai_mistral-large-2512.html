## 1. Trajectories

### **1. AI-Driven Abundance (30%)**
**Causal mechanism:** AI and automation advance rapidly (AGI or near-AGI by 2035), leading to extreme productivity gains, near-zero marginal cost production, and a post-scarcity economy for essentials. Governments adapt via universal basic services, citizen dividends, or aggressive redistribution. Labor demand collapses in routine and cognitive jobs, but new human-centric roles emerge in care, creativity, and governance.

**Median person’s life in 2046:**
- **Income sources:** 40% from government transfers (UBI or dividends), 30% from part-time or gig work (art, coaching, local services), 20% from asset ownership (housing, AI royalties), 10% from informal barter.
- **Work:** Works 15-20 hours/week, often in roles requiring emotional intelligence or niche expertise. Many "jobs" are hobbies monetized via micropayments or community currencies.
- **Costs:** Housing is cheap (3D-printed or modular homes dominate), food is near-free (vertical farms + lab-grown), energy is abundant (fusion or advanced solar). Healthcare is universal and AI-managed.
- **Security:** High. Basic needs are guaranteed, but status competition shifts to non-material domains (reputation, creativity, social capital).

### **2. Stagnant Oligarchy (25%)**
**Causal mechanism:** AI progresses steadily but is captured by incumbents (corporations, states, or tech elites). Productivity gains accrue to capital, while labor is deskilled or displaced without compensation. Governments fail to adapt, leading to a two-tier economy: a wealthy class of asset owners and a precarious underclass. Political power consolidates, and democracy erodes.

**Median person’s life in 2046:**
- **Income sources:** 50% from gig work (deliveries, content moderation, micro-tasks), 20% from government welfare (means-tested and stigmatized), 20% from informal economy (side hustles, gray markets), 10% from family support.
- **Work:** Works 30-40 hours/week, often in low-paid, AI-supervised roles. Job churn is high; skills atrophy quickly. Many work multiple jobs to afford basics.
- **Costs:** Housing is expensive (NIMBYism + corporate landlords), food and energy costs are volatile (geopolitical shocks, supply chain fragility). Healthcare is privatized and rationed.
- **Security:** Low. Welfare is conditional and punitive. Automation hollows out the middle class, leaving a small elite and a large precariat. Protests are common but ineffective.

### **3. Green Degrowth (20%)**
**Causal mechanism:** Climate disasters and resource constraints force a deliberate contraction of high-consumption economies. AI is deployed selectively (e.g., for efficiency, not growth), and policy prioritizes sustainability over GDP. Labor shifts to local production, care work, and maintenance. Wealth is redistributed to fund public goods, but material living standards decline for most.

**Median person’s life in 2046:**
- **Income sources:** 40% from government jobs (public healthcare, education, infrastructure), 30% from local cooperative work (farming, repair, crafts), 20% from transfers (universal basic services), 10% from informal trade.
- **Work:** Works 25-30 hours/week, often in physically demanding or community-oriented roles. Automation is limited to essential tasks (e.g., energy management). Job satisfaction is high, but pay is low.
- **Costs:** Housing is affordable (strict zoning + public housing), but quality is basic. Food is expensive (organic/local production), energy is rationed (carbon taxes). Imports are rare; most goods are locally made.
- **Security:** Moderate. Basic needs are met, but choice is limited. Social cohesion is strong, but innovation is slow. Inequality is low, but absolute living standards are lower than in 2026.

### **4. AI Winter + Secular Stagnation (15%)**
**Causal mechanism:** AI progress stalls after 2030 (diminishing returns, alignment failures, or regulatory overreach). Productivity growth remains sluggish, and demographic decline accelerates. Governments and firms lack tools to escape stagnation, leading to persistent low growth, high debt, and austerity. Labor demand shrinks slowly, but without a productivity revolution.

**Median person’s life in 2046:**
- **Income sources:** 50% from traditional employment (service, clerical, or manual work), 30% from government pensions or welfare, 15% from side hustles, 5% from family support.
- **Work:** Works 35-40 hours/week in a job similar to 2026 (e.g., nurse, teacher, retail). Automation is incremental (e.g., better spreadsheets, not AGI). Job security is low; layoffs are common.
- **Costs:** Housing is expensive (no new supply, aging population), food and energy costs rise slowly (inflation outpaces wages). Healthcare is underfunded and rationed.
- **Security:** Low. Stagnant wages, high debt, and weak safety nets. The economy feels like a slower, grimmer version of the 2010s.

---

### **5. Fragmented Collapse (10%)**
**Causal mechanism:** AI accelerates inequality and instability, leading to societal breakdown. Automation displaces labor faster than new jobs emerge, while governments fail to redistribute gains. Climate shocks, pandemics, or AI-driven misinformation trigger mass unrest. Economies splinter into city-states, corporate enclaves, and failed regions.

**Median person’s life in 2046:**
- **Income sources:** 40% from barter or gray markets, 30% from local warlord/corporate patronage, 20% from scavenging or theft, 10% from remnants of government welfare.
- **Work:** Works irregularly, often in dangerous or exploitative conditions (e.g., guarding supply chains, manual labor for corporate zones). Many are unemployed or underemployed.
- **Costs:** Housing is informal (squatting, refugee camps). Food and energy are scarce and expensive. Healthcare is nonexistent outside elite zones.
- **Security:** Very low. Violence, disease, and starvation are common. The state is weak or predatory. Trust in institutions is gone.

## 3. Drivers

1. **AI productivity acceleration**
   - *Why it matters:* Determines whether automation complements or replaces labor, and whether growth is broad-based or captured by elites.
   - *Trajectory:* Accelerating until 2035, then plateauing due to alignment challenges or diminishing returns. 60% chance of near-AGI by 2040.

2. **Political capacity for redistribution**
   - *Why it matters:* Without redistribution, AI-driven productivity gains will flow to capital, worsening inequality.
   - *Trajectory:* Declining. 70% chance that by 2046, governments lack the fiscal or political tools to tax/redistribute AI rents effectively.

3. **Climate and resource constraints**
   - *Why it matters:* Could force degrowth or collapse trajectories, overriding AI-driven abundance.
   - *Trajectory:* Increasingly binding. 50% chance of a major climate-driven economic shock (e.g., food/energy crisis) by 2040.

4. **Labor demand elasticity**
   - *Why it matters:* If new jobs emerge as fast as old ones disappear, stagnation is avoided. If not, unemployment or precarity rises.
   - *Trajectory:* Low elasticity. 60% chance that labor demand shrinks by >20% in developed economies by 2046.

5. **Demographic decline**
   - *Why it matters:* Aging populations reduce growth, increase dependency ratios, and strain welfare systems.
   - *Trajectory:* Accelerating. 80% chance that working-age populations in developed economies shrink by >10% by 2046.

6. **Institutional adaptability**
   - *Why it matters:* Can governments and firms retool for an AI economy, or do they ossify?
   - *Trajectory:* Low adaptability. 70% chance that by 2046, institutions are less capable of handling shocks than in 2026.

7. **Energy breakthroughs**
   - *Why it matters:* Cheap, abundant energy could enable AI-driven abundance; scarcity could force degrowth.
   - *Trajectory:* Mixed. 50% chance of fusion or advanced solar by 2040, but 30% chance of energy rationing due to climate policy.

8. **AI alignment success**
   - *Why it matters:* If AI systems are misaligned or weaponized, they could destabilize economies or governments.
   - *Trajectory:* Partial success. 40% chance of a major AI-driven catastrophe (e.g., financial meltdown, misinformation crisis) by 2046.

## 4. The mechanism you'd bet on

The single most predictive mechanism for the median person’s economic welfare in 2046 is **whether AI-driven productivity gains are broadly distributed or captured by a narrow elite**. If governments can tax and redistribute the rents from AI (via UBI, public services, or citizen dividends), the median person will likely be better off—even if labor demand collapses. If not, the gains will flow to capital, and the median person will face stagnation or precarity, regardless of how fast AI advances. The key variable is political: can democracies or autocracies adapt their tax and welfare systems faster than AI concentrates wealth? History suggests they cannot, but the stakes are higher this time.

## 5. Confidence and failure

**Evidence that would make my central forecast wrong:**
- **AI progress stalls:** If AGI or near-AGI fails to materialize by 2040, the "AI Winter" trajectory becomes dominant.
- **Political revolution:** If a major developed economy (e.g., US, EU) implements a robust UBI or citizen dividend by 2035, the "AI-Driven Abundance" scenario becomes more likely.
- **Climate collapse:** If a runaway climate event (e.g., Gulf Stream collapse, mass crop failures) occurs before 2040, the "Green Degrowth" or "Fragmented Collapse" trajectories dominate.
- **Energy breakthrough:** If fusion or advanced solar becomes viable and scalable by 2035, the "AI-Driven Abundance" scenario becomes more plausible.

**Least confident claims:**
1. The probability of **median real disposable income being higher in 2046 (60%)**. This hinges on political will for redistribution, which is hard to predict.
2. The probability of **AI systems making most resource-allocation decisions (60%)**. This depends on whether AI is deployed in a decentralized or centralized manner, which is unclear.
3. The **30% probability of >25% prime-age unemployment**. This assumes labor demand elasticity is low, but new job categories could emerge (e.g., AI trainers, climate adaptation roles).
4. The **35% probability of government transfers being the largest income source for the median household**. This depends on political feasibility, which is highly uncertain.
