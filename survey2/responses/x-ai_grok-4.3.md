# x-ai/grok-4.3

## 1. Trajectories

Accelerating AI deployment occurs through 2035, followed by rapid capability jumps that automate most cognitive and physical routine tasks by 2042.

- Trajectory A – Broad productivity dividend (probability 30%): Strong diffusion of AI tools, competitive markets, and modest redistribution. Median person in 2046 earns primary income from part-time oversight or niche creative roles supplemented by small citizen dividend; housing and energy costs fall in real terms due to automated construction and generation; economic security is moderate, with frequent job switches but low material deprivation.
- Trajectory B – Ownership concentration (probability 35%): Frontier AI controlled by a handful of firms and investors; labor share collapses but aggregate output rises. Median person receives most income from means-tested transfers or low-skill service gigs; work consists of unpredictable platform tasks; living costs stabilize rather than decline; security is low, with periodic benefit cuts tied to fiscal politics.
- Trajectory C – Regulatory slowdown plus partial automation (probability 20%): Safety rules and compute restrictions slow deployment after 2032. Median person retains conventional employment in regulated sectors such as healthcare, education, and trades; real income grows modestly; housing remains the dominant expense; security resembles today’s middle-income experience with gradual erosion of pensions.
- Trajectory D – Labor displacement without fiscal offset (probability 10%): Fast automation meets weak political response. Median person cycles through short-term gigs and unemployment spells; transfers cover only basics; essential goods prices fall but housing and healthcare do not; insecurity is high, with visible homelessness and emigration pressure.
- Trajectory E – Geopolitical fracture (probability 5%): Major power conflict or sustained export controls fragments AI supply chains. Median person faces stagnant or declining real income, rationed energy, and conscription risk; work is directed toward national priorities; material security deteriorates sharply outside protected enclaves.

## 3. Drivers

- Annual growth rate of effective AI task automation (2026–2035): determines speed of labor displacement versus output gains.
- Compute and data concentration ratio among top five entities: governs whether productivity accrues to broad capital or narrow owners.
- Effective tax rate on AI-derived profits and capital returns: sets fiscal capacity for transfers.
- Regulatory stringency on model training scale (FLOP threshold): controls whether deployment accelerates or plateaus.
- Real housing supply elasticity under automated construction: shapes cost-of-living trajectory for median households.
- Labor-force participation response to wage declines: influences measured unemployment and political pressure for redistribution.
- Cross-border AI chip and model diffusion speed: affects whether developed economies capture rents or face sudden competition shocks.

## 4. The mechanism you'd bet on

The decisive mechanism is the interaction between the rate at which AI substitutes for median-skill tasks and the political elasticity of fiscal redistribution; when substitution is rapid and redistribution remains limited to existing welfare rules, the median household’s consumption growth decouples from GDP growth and becomes dependent on transfer politics rather than market earnings.

## 5. Confidence and failure

Central forecast would be overturned by either sustained technical stagnation after 2030 or by the sudden emergence of cheap, widely distributed robotic hardware that collapses housing and energy costs faster than ownership concentrates. Least confident claims are h (robot price trajectory) and j (degree of autonomous resource allocation), both of which hinge on hardware bottlenecks and corporate governance choices that remain highly uncertain.
